Let me walk through the most compelling evidence. The bull keeps pointing to spectacular trailing numbers—73% revenue growth, 94% net income growth, 71% gross margins. All true. All backward-looking. The problem is we're not investing in what already happened. We're investing in what happens next, and that's where the case falls apart.

The scenario probabilities tell the real story, and the bull is misreading them. Yes, the bull case is 40% probable with 16-20% upside to $220-240. But the base case is 45% probable and implies $180-200—essentially flat from the current $188 price. Add the 15% bear case with 15-25% downside, and you get a 60% probability of making zero to negative returns versus a 40% probability of making mid-teens returns. That's not asymmetric upside. That's a coin flip with downside skew.

The energy constraint argument is the most underappreciated risk in this entire debate. The bull tried to reframe it as "neutral-to-positive" because it extends the demand cycle. That's creative, but wrong. When the Wall Street Journal reports that AI is using so much energy that computing firepower is running out, that's not a timing issue—that's a structural ceiling. You cannot deploy chips you cannot power. Data centers cannot be built where grid capacity doesn't exist. This is physics, not sentiment. And when energy becomes the binding constraint, customers optimize for performance-per-watt, which opens the door to AMD's lower-power inference chips and custom ASICs. That accelerates exactly the competitive pressure NVDA doesn't need.

On competition, the bull admits NVDA's share will compress from 80% to 70-75% by 2027 but waves it away because TAM is growing 40% annually. Here's the problem: NVDA's valuation premium is built on monopoly assumptions. The moment the market realizes this is no longer a winner-take-all story, the multiple compresses from 38x to 25x or lower—that's a 35% haircut even if earnings stay flat. And we're already seeing the early signs: Bank of America explicitly named AMD and Marvell as top AI picks, not NVDA. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are building their own chips with multi-billion-dollar programs designed to reduce NVDA dependence. This isn't theoretical—it's happening now.

The inventory and accounts receivable increases are red flags the bull completely ignored. Inventory up 8.2% quarter-over-quarter to $21.4 billion, and AR up 15.2% to $38.5 billion. If demand is so insatiable, why is NVDA building inventory? Why are customers stretching payment terms? These are classic signs of demand softening or order pushouts.

Recent relative performance matters more than the bull wants to admit. When money flows into semiconductors, it's going to AMD (up 12.66% in one week) and Broadcom (up 18.12%), not NVDA (up 6.34%). That's not rotation noise—that's loss of market leadership. The technical setup the bull is excited about shows price testing the upper Bollinger Band at $188.59 with RSI at 61. That's not breakout territory, that's overbought exhaustion setting up for a pullback.

The macro risk is real. PPI at 2.08% month-over-month isn't a "monitoring variable"—it's a flashing red light. If producer inflation passes through to CPI, the Fed doesn't stay on hold at 3.64%, they hike. When they hike, the 10-year yield goes from 4.29% to 4.75% or higher, and every high-duration growth stock gets repriced lower. NVDA's forward P/E of 17x assumes rates stay low and growth stays high. Break either assumption and that multiple compresses to 12-14x, putting fair value at $140-160.

Here's the bottom line: the bull case requires everything to go right—hyperscaler capex stays elevated, energy infrastructure catches up, competition stays slow, margins stay above 70%, and valuation multiples hold or expand. The bear case only requires one or two things to go wrong—hyperscaler capex decelerates 10-15%, energy constraints bite harder, AMD wins a few more design wins, or the Fed hikes once. Any one of those breaks the bull thesis.
