## Macroeconomic Analysis – April 13, 2026

### Regime Identification: **Soft Landing in Progress**

The Fed has delivered ~45 basis points of cuts from October 2025 (4.09%) through January 2026, then held steady at 3.64% for three consecutive months. This pause at a near-neutral rate, combined with a healthy labor market (unemployment 4.3%, down 10bp; payrolls +178k) and a normalized yield curve (2Y-10Y +51bp), signals the Fed believes it has engineered a soft landing.

The Treasury curve is upward-sloping across all maturities, with the 10Y at 4.29% and the 30Y at 4.90%. This is consistent with normal growth expectations and no imminent recession signal. The VIX at 19.23 (down 1.3%) reflects calm, not complacency.

### Critical Data Gap: Inflation Blind Spot

**CPI and PPI data failed to load.** This is a material risk. Without inflation readings, we cannot confirm whether:
- The Fed's pause is justified or premature
- Disinflation is stalling
- Wage-price dynamics are re-accelerating

The committee should treat this as a **known unknown** and assign higher uncertainty to any rate-path assumptions.

### Policy Path & Cross-Asset Implications

**Fed stance:** On hold, likely data-dependent. If inflation (which we can't see) is cooperating, the next move is probably another cut in H2 2026. If inflation is sticky, the pause extends.

**Rates:** The curve's steepness (3.68% at 3M → 4.90% at 30Y) suggests bond markets expect growth to persist and the Fed to remain cautious. Long-duration assets face headwinds if growth surprises to the upside.

**Equities:** Soft landing is the Goldilocks scenario—growth without overheating. Cyclicals and industrials benefit. However, any inflation surprise that forces the Fed to hike would be a sharp negative catalyst.

**Credit:** Low VIX and stable rates support tight spreads. Default risk remains low in this environment.

**Commodities (relevant for ALB):** Industrial metals and materials benefit from sustained growth. However, lithium is a special case—driven more by EV adoption, China policy, and supply gluts than by broad macro. The macro backdrop is supportive but not determinative.

### Risks to Monitor

1. **Inflation resurgence** (data unavailable—highest priority to resolve)
2. **Labor market cracks** (currently strong, but watch for sudden weakening)
3. **Curve inversion** (not present, but a steepener-to-flattener move would signal growth concerns)
4. **Geopolitical shocks** (not in data, but always a tail risk)
