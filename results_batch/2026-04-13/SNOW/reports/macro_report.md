## Macro Regime Assessment: Soft Landing in Progress

**Regime:** Late-cycle normalization with disinflationary tailwinds. The Fed has delivered ~45 bps of cuts since October 2025 and is now on hold at 3.64%, consistent with a neutral-to-slightly-restrictive stance. Growth remains resilient (payrolls +178k, unemployment 4.3%), inflation is cooling at the consumer level (CPI +0.87% m/m) but producer prices remain sticky (PPI +2.08% m/m), and the Treasury curve is positively sloped (+51 bps 2s10s), signaling no imminent recession risk. VIX at 19.23 reflects contained volatility and stable risk appetite.

**Policy Path:** The Fed appears to be in wait-and-see mode. With unemployment low and payrolls steady, there is no urgency to cut further unless inflation undershoots or growth deteriorates. The elevated PPI print suggests input-cost pressures may limit the Fed's dovish flexibility. Expect data-dependent policy through mid-2026, with cuts resuming only if labor softens or disinflation accelerates.

**Cross-Asset Implications:**
- **Equities:** Supportive backdrop for risk assets. Stable rates, healthy growth, and low volatility favor cyclicals and growth stocks, particularly those sensitive to enterprise spending and cloud adoption.
- **Rates:** The curve's positive slope and 10Y at 4.29% suggest term premium is normalizing. Duration risk is moderate; long-end yields may drift higher if growth surprises or fiscal concerns resurface.
- **Credit:** Benign default environment. Spreads should remain tight absent an exogenous shock.
- **FX/Commodities:** Dollar likely range-bound; commodity demand supported by stable growth but capped by moderating inflation.

**Key Risks:**
1. **PPI persistence:** If producer inflation feeds through to consumer prices, the Fed may hold rates higher for longer, pressuring growth-sensitive equities.
2. **Labor market inflection:** A sharp rise in unemployment would trigger dovish repricing and volatility.
3. **Curve steepening:** If long-end yields rise faster than short-end cuts, financing costs for growth companies could increase.
4. **Geopolitical/fiscal shocks:** Not priced in; any surprise could spike VIX and compress risk appetite.
