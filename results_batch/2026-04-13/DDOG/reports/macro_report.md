## Macroeconomic Analysis – April 13, 2026

### Regime: Reflationary Pause with Hawkish Tilt

The Fed cut 45 bps between October 2025 and January 2026 (from 4.09% to 3.64%), then paused for three consecutive months. That pause now looks prescient: **inflation is reaccelerating sharply**. CPI rose 0.87% month-over-month in March, and PPI surged 2.08% in February—both well above the Fed's comfort zone. Meanwhile, the labor market remains tight: unemployment fell to 4.30% and payrolls added 178k jobs in March.

The **Treasury curve is upward-sloping** (6M at 3.71%, 7Y at 4.10%), signaling no recession fears and modest term-premium demand. The absence of inversion confirms the market expects continued growth, not contraction.

### Policy Path & Cross-Asset Implications

- **Fed stance**: On hold indefinitely. If inflation prints remain hot, the next move is a hike, not a cut. The market is underpricing this risk.
- **Rates**: The curve will likely steepen further if growth stays firm and inflation persists. Front-end yields (2Y–3Y) are vulnerable to hawkish repricing.
- **Equities**: Reflationary environments favor cyclicals and value over duration-sensitive growth—**but** if the Fed stays paused (not hiking), tech/growth can still run on earnings momentum. A hike would be a headwind.
- **Credit**: Spreads should remain tight in a no-recession scenario, but rising rates cap total return.

### Risks for Other Analysts

1. **Inflation persistence**: If CPI/PPI stay elevated, the Fed may hike in Q2/Q3 2026, repricing the entire curve higher.
2. **Labor tightness**: Wage pressure could keep core inflation sticky, forcing the Fed's hand.
3. **Curve steepening**: Long-duration assets (growth equities, long bonds) face valuation pressure if the 10Y pushes above 4.5%.
4. **No recession signal**: The inverted-curve recession playbook is off the table. Focus on overheating risk, not slowdown risk.
