## Macro Regime: Mid-Cycle Pause with Inflation Watch

**Growth & Labor**: The economy is running at a steady, moderate pace. Nonfarm payrolls added 178k in March, unemployment ticked down to 4.3%, and the labor market remains tight but not overheating. GDP data is stale (Q4 2025), but the employment picture suggests continued expansion without acceleration.

**Inflation**: This is where the story gets interesting. Headline CPI rose 0.87% month-over-month in March—annualized that's over 10%, though sequential volatility is common. More concerning: PPI jumped 2.08% sequentially in February, signaling upstream price pressure that could feed through to consumer prices. The Fed's pause since January now looks less like "mission accomplished" and more like "wait and see."

**Policy Path**: The Fed cut 45bp from October 2025 to January 2026 (4.09% → 3.64%), then held steady for three months. At 3.64%, policy is near the lower end of neutral estimates (3.5–4.5%). The curve agrees: the 2Y-10Y spread is +51bp, a normal upward slope consistent with growth expectations and no recession fear. The Fed is done easing for now. If PPI inflation persists or feeds into CPI, the next move could be a hike, not a cut.

**Cross-Asset Implications**:
- **Rates**: The curve is steep and stable. Long-end yields (10Y at 4.29%, 30Y at 4.90%) reflect term premium and growth, not distress. If inflation re-accelerates, expect bear steepening (long yields rise faster than short).
- **Equities**: VIX at 19.23 signals complacency. Earnings can handle 3.64% rates, but a hawkish Fed pivot would reprice risk assets lower. Growth/cyclicals outperform in this regime; defensives and rate-sensitive sectors (utilities, REITs) face headwinds if yields rise.
- **Credit**: Spreads likely tight given low VIX and stable growth. Watch for widening if the Fed signals concern about inflation persistence.
- **FX**: A hawkish Fed pause supports the dollar. EM and commodity currencies vulnerable if the Fed holds longer than markets expect.

**Key Risks**:
1. **Inflation persistence**: PPI's 2% jump is a red flag. If it continues, the Fed's pause becomes a policy error.
2. **Policy mistake**: Cutting too much too fast in late 2025 may have rekindled price pressure. The Fed may need to reverse course.
3. **Curve inversion risk**: If the Fed hikes and the front end rises faster than the long end, inversion could return—a recession warning.
4. **Geopolitical/supply shocks**: Not in the data, but any energy or supply-chain disruption would amplify inflation risk.

**Bottom Line**: We're in a mid-cycle pause with an inflation wildcard. The Fed is on hold, growth is steady, and the curve is normal—but PPI's surge is a canary in the coal mine. If producer prices feed into consumer inflation, the Fed's next move is a hike, not a cut. Risk assets are priced for perfection (low VIX, tight spreads); any hawkish surprise will reprice equities and credit lower. Duration is vulnerable. Favor cyclicals over defensives, but keep powder dry for a potential regime shift if inflation data deteriorates further.

**Recommendation for the committee**: Underweight duration (bonds), neutral equities with a tilt toward value/cyclicals, and monitor the April CPI print closely. If PPI → CPI transmission materializes, pivot defensive.
