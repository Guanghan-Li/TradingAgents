# Macroeconomic Analysis for SCCO Trading Decision
**Date: April 13, 2026**

## Macro Regime Assessment

**Current Regime: Late-Cycle Normalization with Inflation Persistence**

The Federal Reserve has executed 45 basis points of cuts since October 2025, bringing the policy rate to 3.64%, where it has stabilized for three consecutive months. This pause signals the Fed believes it has reached a neutral-to-slightly-restrictive stance. However, the inflation picture remains mixed and concerning:

- **Headline CPI** rose 0.87% month-over-month in March, an annualized pace near 11% - well above the Fed's 2% target
- **PPI** surged 2.08% month-over-month, suggesting upstream price pressures are building and may flow through to consumers
- The **labor market** remains resilient: unemployment fell to 4.30% with 178k payroll additions, indicating no recession signal from employment

## Yield Curve & Growth Outlook

The Treasury curve shows a **+50bp 2Y-10Y spread** - positive but flat. This configuration typically reflects:
- Market pricing in modest long-term growth
- No imminent recession signal (curve is not inverted)
- Term premium remains compressed, suggesting bond investors are not demanding significant compensation for duration risk

The **VIX at 19.23** indicates moderate, manageable volatility - not a stress environment, but elevated from complacency levels.

## Policy Path & Cross-Asset Implications

The Fed appears **on hold indefinitely**. With inflation reaccelerating (especially PPI) and the labor market strong, the next move is more likely a hike than a cut if inflation does not moderate. This creates:

1. **Dollar strength bias**: Higher-for-longer rates support USD
2. **Pressure on commodities priced in USD**: A stronger dollar typically weighs on commodity prices
3. **Equity sector rotation risk**: Late-cycle dynamics favor quality, defensives; cyclicals and materials face headwinds
4. **Credit spread stability**: No recession signal means credit remains supported, but growth-sensitive sectors may underperform

## Risks for SCCO (Southern Copper)

SCCO is a copper producer - a cyclical, commodity-exposed equity. Key macro risks:

- **Inflation persistence** could force the Fed to hike, strengthening USD and pressuring copper prices
- **Flat yield curve** suggests muted long-term growth expectations, reducing industrial metal demand
- **China growth** (not in this dataset but critical for copper) - any slowdown in Chinese infrastructure/manufacturing would hit copper demand
- **Producer price surge** may reflect supply-side constraints (bullish for copper) OR demand-pull inflation that the Fed will crush (bearish for growth and copper)

**Bullish case**: If PPI inflation reflects supply tightness in commodities, copper prices could remain elevated. Strong labor market supports domestic demand.

**Bearish case**: Fed on hold or hiking into slowing growth would strengthen USD, weaken copper prices, and compress margins for producers.

## Bottom Line for SCCO

**Macro backdrop is MIXED-TO-NEGATIVE for copper equities:**
- Fed policy is restrictive and may tighten further if inflation does not cool
- Flat yield curve signals limited long-term growth optimism
- Strong USD environment is a headwind for dollar-denominated commodities
- PPI surge is ambiguous: could reflect commodity supply tightness (bullish) or broad inflation the Fed will fight (bearish for growth)

**Recommendation for macro input to trading decision**: 
- **If copper-specific fundamentals (supply disruptions, China stimulus) are strong**, the macro headwinds may be offset
