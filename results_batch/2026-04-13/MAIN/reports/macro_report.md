**Macro Regime: Late-Cycle Normalization with Inflation Persistence**

The Fed has delivered 125bp of cuts since October 2025, bringing the funds rate to 3.64% and holding steady for three months. This pause signals the committee believes it's near neutral. The upward-sloping yield curve (2Y-10Y +51bp) confirms the market expects neither recession nor aggressive further easing—a "soft landing" baseline.

**Inflation & Growth Backdrop:**
- **CPI** rose 0.87% month-over-month in March—annualizes to ~11%, well above target and likely reflecting base effects or a temporary spike.
- **PPI** surged 2.08% sequentially, signaling upstream price pressure that may feed through to consumer prices.
- **Labor market** remains firm: unemployment ticked down to 4.30%, payrolls added 178k. No signs of slack that would justify further cuts.

**Policy Path:**
The Fed is on hold. With inflation sticky and labor tight, the next move is more likely a hike than a cut if CPI remains elevated. The curve's steepness (30Y at 4.90%) suggests bond vigilantes are pricing long-term inflation risk or term premium.

**Cross-Asset Implications:**
- **Equities:** Late-cycle dynamics favor quality over cyclicals. Sticky inflation + higher-for-longer rates compress multiples, especially for long-duration growth.
- **Bonds:** Front-end anchored near 3.65%, but long-end vulnerable if inflation doesn't cool. Curve steepening = bearish duration.
- **Credit:** Spreads should widen modestly as the cycle matures; default risk rises if the Fed is forced to tighten.
- **FX:** Dollar strength likely if the Fed holds while other central banks ease.

**Key Risks:**
1. **Inflation re-acceleration** forces the Fed to hike, inverting the curve and triggering recession.
2. **Policy error:** If the Fed cuts prematurely, inflation becomes entrenched.
3. **Exogenous shock** (geopolitical, supply chain) that disrupts the soft-landing narrative.
