FCX macro view: the U.S. backdrop looks like a late-cycle soft-landing rather than an imminent recession. Labor is still holding up, with unemployment at 4.3% in March 2026 and payrolls still expanding, while the Treasury curve is positively sloped with a 2Y-10Y spread of +53 bp. That argues against an immediate recession signal and is generally supportive for cyclical equities like FCX.

The complication for FCX is inflation. Headline CPI rose 0.87% sequentially in March and PPI rose 1.78%, which suggests goods and input-cost pressure is still live rather than fully normalized. The Fed has already eased from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by January 2026, but has been on hold since then. With inflation sticky and labor not breaking, the most likely near-term policy path is a prolonged pause, not an aggressive cutting cycle. That keeps real rates relatively firm and limits valuation expansion for miners.

For FCX specifically, this is a mixed-to-slightly constructive macro setup. The positive curve and steady labor market support the industrial demand side of copper, which matters more than domestic recession hedging. But higher long-end yields, sticky inflation, and a Fed pause raise the risk of a firmer dollar and tighter financial conditions, which can pressure metals and mining equities. In practice, FCX screens as more of a growth-sensitive cyclical than a pure rate-cut beneficiary here.

Cross-asset implications for FCX:
- Bullish inputs: no yield-curve recession warning, ongoing payroll growth, policy already meaningfully less restrictive than late 2025.
- Bearish inputs: inflation re-acceleration, long-end yields near 4.3% to 4.9%, and a likely Fed hold that could cap upside in global cyclicals.
- Key external variable: FCX still depends heavily on ex-U.S. industrial demand, especially China-linked copper demand. The U.S. macro tape alone is not enough for a full bull case.

Bottom line for FCX: macro regime is not recessionary, which is supportive, but it is also not clean disinflation plus easing. That leaves FCX in a conditional risk-on regime: constructive if global growth and copper demand stay firm, vulnerable if sticky inflation pushes yields and the dollar higher.
