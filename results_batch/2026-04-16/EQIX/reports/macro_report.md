Macro view for `EQIX` as of 2026-04-16: the US backdrop still looks like a soft-landing regime, not a recession regime. Labor is holding up, with unemployment at 4.3% in March 2026 and nonfarm payrolls still expanding by 178k sequentially, while the effective fed funds rate has already been cut materially from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by March 2026. That combination usually supports risk assets and cyclical demand, which is constructive for `EQIX` operationally because enterprise, cloud, and AI infrastructure demand tends to hold up better when growth remains positive and financing conditions gradually ease.

The inflation picture is less clean. CPI rose 0.87% sequentially in March 2026 and PPI rose 1.78% sequentially, which suggests upstream price pressure has re-accelerated even as the Fed has already moved rates down. So the most likely policy interpretation is not an aggressive easing cycle from here, but a pause-or-slower-cuts regime while the Fed checks whether inflation is stabilizing. That matters for `EQIX` because it is a long-duration, rate-sensitive real-asset equity: lower front-end rates help funding and sentiment, but a sticky inflation impulse can keep longer-dated Treasury yields elevated and cap further multiple expansion.

The yield curve is the key signal. On 2026-04-15, the 2Y Treasury yielded 3.76% and the 10Y yielded 4.29%, leaving a positive 2Y-10Y spread of 53 bps. The curve is no longer inverted, which reduces the classic recession warning and supports a normal-growth interpretation. But the level of long rates is still meaningfully high, with the 30Y at 4.89%. For `EQIX`, that is a mixed macro input: it lowers near-term recession risk and supports leasing demand, but it also keeps pressure on REIT valuation frameworks, cap-rate comparisons, and the cost of long-horizon capital.

Net macro takeaway for `EQIX`: the backdrop is moderately constructive for fundamentals but only mixed for valuation. If other analysts are building the committee view, they should probably treat `EQIX` as benefiting from stable growth and an already-eased Fed, while still facing a nontrivial long-end yield headwind. The main macro risk is a re-acceleration in inflation that prevents additional cuts and keeps the 10Y and 30Y elevated; the main macro upside is that disinflation resumes while growth stays intact, which would be especially helpful for `EQIX` because that would support both operating demand and REIT multiple expansion.
