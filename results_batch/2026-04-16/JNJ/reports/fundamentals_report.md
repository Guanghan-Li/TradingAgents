JNJ fundamental update as of 2026-04-16

Johnson & Johnson is a large-cap defensive healthcare company in Drug Manufacturers - General with a market cap of about $565.2B. The core fundamental picture is high quality: TTM revenue is $96.36B, gross profit $65.52B, EBITDA $34.10B, net income $21.04B, profit margin 21.83%, and operating margin 26.72%. Beta is 0.329, which fits JNJ’s lower-volatility, defensive profile. Valuation is not distressed, but it is also not extreme for a high-quality healthcare compounder: trailing P/E is 21.26x, forward P/E 18.47x, price/book 6.93x, TTM EPS $11.03, forward EPS $12.70. That combination implies the market is still paying for stability, cash generation, and earnings durability.

On recent operating momentum, 2025 quarterly revenue improved through the year: $21.89B in 2025-03-31, $23.74B in 2025-06-30, $23.99B in 2025-09-30, and $24.56B in 2025-12-31. Reported Q1 2025 net income was unusually high at $10.999B because of large non-operating/special items, including $6.883B of total unusual items, so traders should not annualize that quarter. A cleaner read is normalized income: about $5.44B in Q1 2025, $5.93B in Q2 2025, $4.99B in Q3 2025, and $5.72B in Q4 2025. That suggests the earnings base is stable-to-improving, but headline GAAP prints are distorted by one-offs. Normalized EBITDA was also steady at a high level: $8.72B, $9.21B, $9.28B, and $7.96B across 2025 quarters, with Q4 still healthy despite a $765M unusual item drag.

Profitability remains strong. Q4 2025 gross profit was $16.60B on $24.56B revenue, implying gross margin of roughly 67.6%. Operating income was $5.59B, and R&D was $4.25B in the quarter, showing JNJ is still supporting innovation while preserving margin. Across 2025, quarterly R&D ranged from $3.23B to $4.25B, which is material but manageable relative to gross profit. SG&A remains large, but that is normal for JNJ’s scale and global commercial footprint.

Balance sheet quality is good, but traders should read through the accounting mix. At 2025-12-31, JNJ had $199.21B of total assets, $81.54B of equity, and $47.93B of total debt, with net debt of $28.22B against about $19.71B of cash and equivalents. Debt/equity in the prefetched summary is 60.499, which signals leverage is meaningful but still supportable given cash generation and margins. The main balance sheet caution is that goodwill and other intangibles are very large at $99.18B, which drives tangible book value negative at -$17.63B. That is not unusual for acquisitive pharma/medtech businesses, but it means book value is less informative and future impairment risk should be monitored if acquired assets underperform.

Liquidity is adequate, though working capital tightened versus early 2025. Working capital fell from $14.65B on 2025-03-31 to $1.50B on 2025-12-31. Current assets were $55.62B versus current liabilities of $54.13B at year-end, so the current position is still positive but less cushioned than earlier in the year. Inventory rose to $14.19B from $12.44B at 2024-12-31, and receivables increased to $21.33B from $18.93B over the same period. Those are not alarming on their own, but they are worth watching because they can affect cash conversion.

Cash flow remains one of the strongest parts of the JNJ story. Quarterly operating cash flow in 2025 was $4.17B, $3.88B, $9.17B, and $7.31B. Free cash flow was $3.37B, $2.48B, $8.00B, and $5.47B. The weaker Q1/Q2 profile was driven partly by working-capital pressure and acquisition/investing activity, not clear operating deterioration. JNJ continued funding shareholder returns with cash dividends of roughly $3.13B per quarter in the second half of 2025 and buybacks of about $1.9B in both Q3 and Q4. That is important for traders: the dividend looks well supported by FCF, but buyback intensity appears opportunistic rather than aggressive.

The biggest cash flow event in 2025 was acquisition/investing activity. In Q2 2025, JNJ used $14.46B for business purchases, contributing to a large investing outflow of $18.26B and a $20.05B drop in cash that quarter. Goodwill and intangible assets also stepped up meaningfully from Q1 to Q2 2025, which is consistent with M&A. This matters because near-term earnings quality depends partly on how those acquired assets integrate and whether management can convert them into durable revenue growth without later impairments.

Capital structure is manageable. Long-term debt at 2025-12-31 was $39.44B, current debt $8.50B, and cash/equivalents plus short-term investments $20.10B. JNJ can service this easily with current EBITDA and cash flow. Interest burden is modest relative to earnings: Q4 2025 interest expense was $214M against EBIT of $5.18B. In a still-positive yield environment, that low-beta, strong-margin profile remains attractive for defensive positioning.

Market behavior versus fundamentals is constructive. JNJ was down 2.81% in the last week and underperformed XLV by 0.98%, so short-term price action softened. But that weakness sits inside a much stronger medium-term tape: +7.89% over 3 months, +24.33% over 6 months, and +13.71% YTD, versus XLV at -6.74%, +3.86%, and -5.34%, respectively. Peer rank is 2/8 over 3 months. That tells traders JNJ has already been recognized as a relative winner in healthcare. The implication is that the stock likely has less room for multiple expansion from here unless earnings keep confirming.

Macro context is mixed but generally supportive for JNJ. As of 2026-04-16, the effective Fed funds rate is 3.64%, the 2Y Treasury is 3.76%, the 10Y is 4.29%, CPI is still rising, and the yield curve is normally upward sloping. For JNJ, that backdrop is acceptable: healthcare demand is less cyclical, and lower-rate volatility plus a VIX near 18 helps support defensive large-cap quality stocks. The main macro risk is not demand collapse; it is valuation compression if long rates keep rising while earnings growth stalls.

Actionable fundamental takeaways for traders:

1. JNJ’s core business looks fundamentally sound. Revenue trend improved through 2025, margins remain strong, and normalized earnings are more stable than noisy GAAP net income suggests.
2. Cash generation supports the investment case. FCF is comfortably positive, dividends look secure, and leverage is serviceable.
3. The main balance sheet caveat is intangible intensity. Negative tangible book is not a liquidity problem today, but it increases sensitivity to acquisition execution and impairment risk.
4. The stock has already outperformed the sector meaningfully over 3, 6, and YTD periods. That reduces the margin for disappointment on future quarters.
5. Near-term weakness of -2.81% over 1 week looks more like consolidation than a breakdown unless new data show deterioration in post-acquisition integration, working capital, or margin.

What to monitor next for JNJ:

- Whether quarterly revenue can stay near or above the Q4 2025 run rate of $24.56B.
- Whether normalized income stays in the roughly $5B to $6B quarterly band.
- Whether FCF remains above dividend outlays after acquisition integration costs.
- Whether working capital rebuilds from the year-end compressed level.
- Whether goodwill/intangible balances lead to future charges.
- Whether valuation stays justified if sector rotation moves away from defensive healthcare.

Bottom line: JNJ remains a high-quality, lower-volatility healthcare name with strong profitability, resilient cash flow, and manageable leverage. The bullish case is based on earnings durability and defense; the cautionary case is that the stock is no longer cheap, and acquisition-heavy accounting makes normalized analysis essential.
