Macro view for `VICI`: the backdrop is best described as a soft-landing / mid-cycle normalization regime, not a recession regime. Growth is still intact, labor remains firm, the Fed has already delivered a meaningful easing sequence into early 2026, and the Treasury curve has re-steepened to a clearly positive slope. That combination is generally supportive for risk assets and tenant-level operating fundamentals, which matters for `VICI` because its rent durability ultimately depends on healthy gaming and experiential property cash flows.

The main complication is inflation. March CPI rose 0.87% sequentially and PPI rose 1.78% sequentially in the prefetched data, which is too hot to read as a clean disinflation continuation. Meanwhile unemployment improved to 4.3% and payrolls still increased by 178k, so the labor backdrop is not weak enough to force rapid additional Fed easing. My base case is that the Fed is now closer to a pause than to an aggressive cutting path: policy has moved from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by March 2026, and with growth holding up plus inflation re-accelerating at the margin, the likely near-term stance is data-dependent hold with only limited further easing unless labor weakens.

For `VICI`, that is a mixed but still modestly constructive macro setup. The constructive side is that lower front-end rates versus late 2025 reduce refinancing pressure and usually help REIT sentiment, while a normal upward-sloping curve and subdued VIX near 18 argue against an imminent hard-landing scare. The offset is that the long end remains elevated, with the 10Y at 4.29% and 30Y near 4.89%. For a long-duration income vehicle like `VICI`, high long rates can cap valuation expansion, keep equity risk premia tight, and slow the extent to which falling policy rates translate into cheaper capital. So the macro message is not "all clear for REIT multiple expansion"; it is "stable growth and easier policy are supportive, but long-bond yields are still a valuation headwind."

Cross-asset implications other analysts should incorporate for `VICI`:
- Rates: front-end easing helps financing conditions, but the still-high 10Y and 30Y matter more for REIT valuation multiples and acquisition economics.
- Credit: a soft-landing backdrop is favorable for tenant credit quality and rent coverage, especially if consumer discretionary spending remains resilient.
- Equities: a positive curve and contained volatility support carry and defensive income sectors, but rate-sensitive equities can lag if long yields keep drifting higher.
- Consumer cyclicals: stable employment supports casino visitation and experiential spend, which is indirectly supportive for `VICI` tenant health.

Concrete macro risks for `VICI`:
- Inflation persistence keeps the Fed on hold longer than equity markets expect, preventing further relief in financing conditions.
- A rise in long Treasury yields from already elevated levels compresses REIT valuations even if operating fundamentals stay solid.
- If the current steep curve reflects rising term premium rather than stronger real growth, `VICI` could face a higher discount-rate environment without a matching improvement in property values.
- If labor softens later in 2026 after the Fed pauses, tenant fundamentals could weaken before rates fall enough to offset that pressure.

Bottom line for `VICI`: macro is mildly supportive for fundamentals but only moderately supportive for valuation. I would frame `VICI` as benefiting more from the soft-landing / lower-front-end-rate environment than being hurt by recession risk, but the still-high long end means the macro case is better for cash-flow stability than for major multiple re-rating.
