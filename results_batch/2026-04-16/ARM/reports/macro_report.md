Macro view for `ARM`: the backdrop is a soft-landing/disinflation regime, not a recession regime. Labor is still holding up, unemployment at 4.3% improved sequentially, payrolls are still growing, and the Treasury curve is positively sloped with 2Y-10Y at +53 bps. That argues against an imminent hard-growth scare. The main macro complication is that inflation is not fully extinguished: CPI and especially PPI both re-accelerated sequentially into March, while the Fed has already eased materially from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by March 2026 and is now on hold.

For `ARM`, that mix is moderately constructive for fundamentals but less clean for valuation. A still-expanding economy supports semiconductor and compute demand, which is helpful for `ARM`'s royalty and licensing exposure. But a steeper long end, with the 10Y at 4.29% and 30Y near 4.89%, keeps duration pressure on long-multiple growth equities. In practice, that means `ARM` can outperform if AI/semis earnings momentum stays strong, but it remains vulnerable to any renewed inflation scare that pushes real yields higher.

Policy path: the Fed looks closer to a wait-and-see stance than an active easing cycle now. Cuts already delivered have brought policy near neutral, but the recent CPI/PPI firming reduces the odds of fast additional cuts. Base case is a patient Fed with asymmetric sensitivity to upside inflation surprises. That is a better setup for cyclicals and financial conditions than for aggressive multiple expansion in high-beta growth.

Cross-asset implications for `ARM`:
- Bullish macro case: growth stays intact, inflation cools back down, long yields stabilize or fall, and quality growth/semis rerate higher. `ARM` benefits from both earnings sensitivity and duration relief.
- Neutral/base case: growth remains okay but long yields stay elevated because inflation is sticky. `ARM` fundamentals can hold up, but upside is more likely to require execution and earnings beats rather than pure multiple expansion.
- Bearish macro case: inflation stays hot enough to halt further Fed easing or reprice policy tighter, pushing the 10Y higher. That is the clearest macro risk for `ARM` because expensive growth equities are highly rate-sensitive.

Key risks other analysts should incorporate for `ARM`:
- Sticky goods or producer inflation feeding back into higher long-end yields.
- A Fed pause lasting longer than equity bulls expect.
- Growth holding up enough to keep yields high, which is paradoxically worse for valuation than a mild slowdown.
- Missing macro breadth: PMI and consumer confidence were unavailable here, so conviction on cyclical acceleration is moderate rather than high.
