Technically, `MP` has materially improved. There is still an important nuance: the average structure is improving faster in price than in the moving averages themselves. The `10 EMA` is rising sharply, but it is still slightly below the `50 SMA` (`56.32` versus `56.81`). That tells you this is a real recovery, but still an early-to-middle-stage trend repair rather than a fully mature uptrend stack. The setup is favorable for existing longs, but less favorable for chasing after a vertical recovery that is already trading outside its volatility envelope.

Anyone entering here with a tight stop is vulnerable to getting shaken out by normal daily movement. In practical terms, `MP` is tradable, but it is not a low-volatility grind higher where you can afford sloppy execution.

The raw tape adds another useful nuance. The recovery has been forceful, but not obviously euphoric. Labor and payroll data do not point to recession, and the positively sloped `2Y-10Y` curve reduces near-term demand-collapse risk. That matters for `MP` because the fundamental macro case is more about industrial and strategic-material demand resilience than about duration-driven multiple expansion.

That is a poor backdrop for assuming a broad valuation rerating. So the macro regime supports trend continuation if the story is cyclical demand and commodity sensitivity, but it does not support paying any price for that exposure. That is exactly why a technically bullish chart can still merit a `HOLD` rather than a `BUY` after a nearly straight-line rebound.
