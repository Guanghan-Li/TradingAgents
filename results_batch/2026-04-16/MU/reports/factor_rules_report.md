MU factor-rule view is balanced but high-stakes: the strongest bullish input is the high-weight AI capex acceleration rule, which would matter most if backlog is still rising and margins are holding despite higher investment, because that would support both growth durability and pricing power. The strongest bearish input is also high-weight: valuation stretch under slowing growth. That rule becomes the key downside risk if forward growth is revised lower while the stock’s multiple remains rich relative to weakening earnings expectations.

The main conflict is that the two highest-weight rules point in opposite directions. In practice, the decision turns on whether AI-driven demand is still translating into backlog, margins, and guidance strength, or whether expectations have outrun fundamentals. The neutral balance-sheet resilience rule is a secondary support factor: it can cushion drawdown risk, but it does not by itself resolve the growth-versus-valuation debate.

Downstream analysts should treat this as a conditional framework, not a conclusion: first verify the Rule 1 operating signals, then test Rule 2 with current valuation and guidance data, and use Rule 3 mainly as a durability overlay if macro conditions are tightening.
