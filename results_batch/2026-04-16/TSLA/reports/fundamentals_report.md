TSLA fundamental report based on the prefetched live fundamentals context dated April 16, 2026.

TSLA remains financially strong on liquidity and balance-sheet resilience, but its current earnings power is still thin relative to its valuation. Trailing 12-month revenue is $94.83B, gross profit is $17.09B, EBITDA is $10.50B, net income is $3.79B, and free cash flow is $3.73B. That translates to a 4.0% net margin, 4.7% operating margin, 4.9% ROE, and 2.1% ROA. Against a market cap of about $1.46T, TSLA is trading at 353.5x TTM earnings, 140.3x forward earnings, and 17.76x book value. The core conclusion is that the balance sheet is robust, but the valuation still assumes a much stronger future earnings trajectory than the company is currently delivering.

From a company-profile standpoint, TSLA is classified in Consumer Cyclical / Auto Manufacturers and still trades as a high-beta equity with beta at 1.915. The stock has been volatile: it is up 12.52% over the last week, but still down 2.60% over 1 month, 11.45% over 3 months, 9.40% over 6 months, and 11.22% YTD. Relative to XLY, TSLA outperformed sharply over 1 week by 8.19 points, but underperformed over every longer tracked period. That matters because the recent bounce looks more like a sentiment move than a broad confirmation of improving fundamentals.

Income statement quality is mixed. Quarterly revenue moved from $19.34B in Q1 2025 to $22.50B in Q2, then $28.10B in Q3, before slipping to $24.90B in Q4 2025. Q4 revenue was down about 3.1% versus Q4 2024 ($25.71B). Gross profit improved materially through 2025, rising from $3.15B in Q1 to $5.01B in Q4. Gross margin improved from about 16.3% in Q1 2025 to about 20.1% in Q4 2025, versus about 16.3% in Q4 2024. That is one of the strongest fundamental positives in the dataset: margins recovered from early-2025 lows.

Operating leverage improved, but not enough to fully justify the equity multiple. Operating income was only $493M in Q1 2025, improved to $923M in Q2, $1.86B in Q3, and $1.57B in Q4. Q4 operating margin was about 6.3%, better than Q1 but still modest for a company carrying TSLA’s market value. R&D rose from $1.28B in Q4 2024 to $1.78B in Q4 2025, while SG&A rose from $1.31B to $1.66B. That spending profile suggests TSLA is still investing aggressively, but it also means cost discipline remains critical if revenue growth stalls.

Net income was more volatile than gross and operating profit. Quarterly net income was $409M in Q1 2025, $1.17B in Q2, $1.37B in Q3, and $840M in Q4. Q4 2025 net income was down about 63.7% from Q4 2024 ($2.31B), even though gross profit improved, because other income swung sharply negative and special items weighed on results. Q4 2025 included total unusual items of -$162M and other income/expense of -$754M. Diluted EPS followed the same pattern: $0.12 in Q1 2025, $0.33 in Q2, $0.39 in Q3, and $0.24 in Q4, versus $0.66 in Q4 2024. For traders, that means the reported earnings line remains fragile even when automotive and operating margins improve.

Cash generation is positive but uneven. Quarterly operating cash flow was $2.16B in Q1 2025, $2.54B in Q2, $6.24B in Q3, and $3.81B in Q4. Free cash flow was $664M, $146M, $3.99B, and $1.42B across those quarters. The business is still producing cash after heavy investment, but cash conversion is lumpy and dependent on working-capital moves. Q3 benefited from a large working-capital tailwind, including a $1.99B inventory benefit, while Q4 showed only a modest $214M working-capital drag. Capex remains elevated at roughly $1.5B to $2.4B per quarter, with Q4 capex at $2.39B. This supports future capacity and infrastructure, but it also means the equity story depends on those investments generating materially higher returns over time.

The balance sheet is the cleanest part of the TSLA fundamental picture. Total assets reached $137.81B at December 31, 2025, up from $122.07B a year earlier. Cash, cash equivalents, and short-term investments were $44.06B, versus total debt of $14.72B, leaving TSLA with very substantial net liquidity. Current ratio is 2.16 and working capital is $36.93B, both consistent with strong short-term solvency. Common equity rose to $82.14B from $72.91B a year earlier. Net PPE increased to $56.18B from $51.51B, and construction in progress rose to $8.79B, showing the company is still deploying capital into its asset base. This gives TSLA strategic flexibility even if industry conditions weaken.

Debt is not the central risk here. The summary field shows debt-to-equity at 17.763, but the underlying balance-sheet data implies total debt of $14.72B against stockholders’ equity of $82.14B, which is closer to about 18% of equity rather than 17.8x equity. Interest expense is low at $85M in Q4 2025, while interest income is $449M, so TSLA is currently earning more on cash than it pays on debt. That is a meaningful advantage in a 3.6% Fed funds environment.

Working-capital and inventory trends are worth monitoring. Inventory ended Q4 2025 at $12.39B, down from the Q2 2025 peak of $14.57B but still slightly above Q4 2024 at $12.02B. Finished goods inventory rose to $4.85B from $3.94B a year earlier. That does not by itself signal distress, but it is consistent with a business that still needs clean sell-through and pricing discipline. Accounts receivable were manageable at $4.58B, while payables and accrued expenses rose to $19.45B, helping finance the operating cycle.

Capital allocation shows continued reinvestment and some dilution pressure. Additional paid-in capital rose to $42.77B from $38.37B a year earlier, and stock-based compensation was $954M in Q4 2025. Proceeds from stock option exercises were $146M in Q4. Diluted average shares rose to 3.539B in Q4 2025 from 3.517B in Q4 2024, so dilution is not exploding, but SBC remains material and should be watched when evaluating per-share earnings quality.

Macro conditions are mixed for TSLA. The effective Fed funds rate was 3.64% as of March 1, 2026, down from late-2025 levels, which is somewhat supportive for auto affordability and financing-sensitive demand. However, CPI and PPI both moved higher sequentially in March 2026, and the front end of the Treasury curve remains around 3.71% to 3.72%. That means financing conditions are easier than before, but not loose. For TSLA, lower rates help at the margin, yet demand elasticity, pricing, and product mix still matter more than the modest recent shift in policy.

Actionable takeaways for traders:

1. The bull case rests on margin expansion, not current earnings. Gross margin recovery toward 20% is real, but net margin at 4.0% and ROE below 5% are still weak relative to valuation.
2. The balance sheet materially reduces bankruptcy or liquidity risk. With $44.06B of cash and short-term investments against $14.72B of debt, downside is cushioned by financial flexibility.
3. Free cash flow is positive but not yet consistently strong enough to match the stock’s multiple. TTM FCF of $3.73B is only about 3.9% of TTM revenue.
4. Recent stock strength is not confirmed by longer-horizon relative performance. TSLA sharply outperformed over 1 week, but underperformed XLY over 1 month, 3 months, 6 months, and YTD.
5. Watch Q1/Q2 2026 for three specific checkpoints: revenue re-acceleration above the mid-$20B quarterly level, gross margin holding near or above 20%, and free cash flow staying solid after capex rather than relying on working-capital timing.
6. Valuation remains the largest fundamental risk. At 353.5x TTM EPS and 17.76x book, even small earnings disappointments can matter a lot.

Bottom line: TSLA is fundamentally liquid, solvent, and still capable of generating positive operating cash flow while continuing heavy investment. The problem is not balance-sheet weakness. The problem is that current profitability is still too low, too volatile, and too dependent on future operating leverage to obviously support the present valuation without further execution improvement.
