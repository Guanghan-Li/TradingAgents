For `SCCO`, the U.S. macro regime on April 16, 2026 looks like a late-cycle soft-landing rather than a recession setup. Labor data is still firm, with unemployment at 4.3% and nonfarm payrolls still expanding, while the Treasury curve is positively sloped with a 2Y-10Y spread of +0.53%. That combination argues against an imminent U.S. demand collapse, which is broadly supportive for cyclical commodity exposure such as `SCCO`.

The offset is inflation. CPI and PPI both re-accelerated in the latest March 2026 readings, while the effective fed funds rate has only eased to 3.64% after a sequence of cuts from late 2025 into early 2026. That suggests the Fed is closer to a pause-than-pivot regime now: policy is no longer overtly restrictive, but sticky price pressure reduces room for aggressive additional easing. For `SCCO`, that is a mixed macro signal: resilient growth supports copper demand, but firmer inflation and higher long-end yields can keep the USD and real rates relatively firm, which can limit upside for industrial metals equities.

Cross-asset implication for `SCCO`: macro is constructive enough to avoid a bearish recession call, but not clean enough for an outright aggressive bullish stance. A normal upward-sloping curve, subdued VIX at 18.17, and a labor market still holding up are positive for cyclical equities and materials. However, the steep long end, with the 10Y at 4.29% and 30Y at 4.89%, signals tighter financial conditions than the policy rate alone implies. That argues for `SCCO` benefiting if copper demand expectations stay firm, but facing valuation and sentiment pressure if inflation stays sticky or if rates reprice upward again.

Key risks the rest of the committee should incorporate for `SCCO`:
- A renewed inflation impulse could delay further Fed easing and strengthen the USD, which would usually be a headwind for copper and copper miners such as `SCCO`.
- The U.S. macro data does not show recession stress yet, but `SCCO` is more globally sensitive than domestically sensitive; if China or broader ex-U.S. industrial demand weakens, U.S. soft-landing data alone will not protect the name.
- The missing ISM manufacturing and consumer-confidence datapoints reduce confidence in the industrial-cycle read, so conviction should stay moderate.

Overall macro takeaway for `SCCO`: the Fed/growth backdrop is supportive enough to avoid a sell, but sticky inflation and elevated long yields keep the regime only moderately favorable. On macro alone, `SCCO` screens as a `HOLD`.
