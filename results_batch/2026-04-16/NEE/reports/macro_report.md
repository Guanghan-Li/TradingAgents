Macro view for `NEE`: the backdrop is a soft-landing / late-cycle expansion, not a recession regime. Labor is still firm (`4.3%` unemployment, `+178k` payrolls), the Fed has already eased from `4.09%` in October 2025 to `3.64%` by March 2026, and the Treasury curve is positively sloped (`2Y-10Y +53 bp`), which argues against an imminent recession signal. The problem is inflation is not fully contained: CPI and especially PPI both moved higher in the latest print, while the long end remains elevated (`10Y 4.29%`, `30Y 4.89%`). That mix implies growth is decent, policy is near neutral, but duration is still expensive.

For `NEE`, that macro mix is mixed-to-slightly negative. As a defensive utility with a large capital program, `NEE` benefits from continued growth, steady power demand, and a lower recession probability, but it is also highly sensitive to long-duration yields, financing costs, and inflation in construction/equipment. A normal curve helps credit functioning, yet elevated long-end yields keep pressure on utility multiples and future-project economics. Base case: the Fed is likely on hold near term unless inflation cools materially; that favors a range-bound macro setup where `NEE` is supported by defensiveness but capped by high real-world funding costs.

Key risks other analysts should incorporate for `NEE`: `1)` sticky inflation pushing the Fed back hawkish and lifting long yields further, `2)` producer-cost inflation squeezing renewable/storage build economics, `3)` any renewed growth slowdown that hurts risk assets broadly, even if utilities outperform on a relative basis, and `4)` duration compression if bond yields fall, which would be the main macro upside catalyst for `NEE`.
