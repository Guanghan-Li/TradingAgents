NEE fundamental continuation report as of 2026-04-16.

**NEE Overview**
NEE is a large-cap regulated electric utility with a market capitalization of about $191.5B. The stock is trading near the upper end of its 52-week range, with a 52-week high of $96.21, low of $63.64, 50-day average of $92.03, and 200-day average of $82.00. That positioning matters because the market is already pricing in a good amount of earnings durability and growth.

Key headline fundamentals remain strong on the surface: TTM revenue of $27.41B, EBITDA of $14.14B, net income of $6.83B, profit margin of 24.93%, and operating margin of 24.45%. For a utility, those are solid profitability figures. Beta is 0.733, which fits the lower-volatility defensive profile traders expect from the sector.

**Valuation and Market Framing**
NEE screens as expensive versus a typical regulated utility base. TTM P/E is 27.83x, forward P/E is 21.02x, and price-to-book is 3.50x. EPS is $3.30 TTM and forward EPS is $4.37, implying the market expects meaningful earnings growth. The forward multiple compressing from 27.8x to 21.0x suggests either real earnings acceleration or that investors are already discounting a better 2026 setup.

For traders, that creates a clear setup:
- If NEE keeps delivering clean earnings growth and rate-base expansion, the premium can hold.
- If growth slips, the valuation leaves room for derating because the stock is not cheap on absolute terms.

The 2.73% dividend yield is respectable, but not high enough by itself to fully offset valuation risk if rates back up again.

This is the most important risk area in the dataset.

At 2025-12-31, NEE reported:
- Total assets: $212.72B
- Total debt: $95.62B
- Net debt: $92.81B
- Common equity: $54.61B
- Total liabilities net minority interest: $146.24B
- Working capital: -$9.23B
- Current ratio: 0.595
- Debt-to-equity: 146.24%

Debt has risen materially over the year:
- Total debt: $82.33B at 2024-12-31 to $95.62B at 2025-12-31
- Net debt: $80.85B to $92.81B over the same period

That is a large increase in leverage in one year, even though equity also rose. Long-term debt climbed from $72.39B to $89.56B. The balance sheet expansion appears tied to capital investment, with net PPE increasing from $138.85B to $156.20B and construction in progress remaining elevated at $24.56B in Q4 2025.

Interpretation: NEE is still building aggressively, but it is financing that growth with a meaningful amount of debt. That is normal to a point for utilities, but it raises sensitivity to interest rates, refinancing conditions, and execution risk.

**Liquidity**
Liquidity is adequate but not comfortable in a conventional industrial sense. Cash at 2025-12-31 was $2.81B, against current liabilities of $22.82B. Current debt was still $6.06B. Negative working capital is common in utilities, but the sub-0.6 current ratio means traders should watch funding access and debt markets closely.

**Actionable Insights for Traders**
1. NEE is fundamentally strong on earnings, but the stock is no longer cheap. Traders should treat it as a premium-quality utility rather than a value utility.
2. The bull case is driven by durable revenue, high margins, forward EPS growth, and strong relative performance.
3. The main risk is leverage plus funding dependence. Debt growth in 2025 was large, and dividends do not appear fully covered by internally generated free cash flow after capex.
4. The balance sheet is still investable, but the market likely needs continued flawless execution to justify 27.8x trailing earnings.
5. Near term, NEE is more exposed to rates than a simple defensive-utility label might suggest because of its capital intensity and debt trajectory.
6. If traders are bullish, the cleaner setup is on pullbacks or after confirmation that long-end yields are stable and financing needs are moderating.
7. If traders are cautious, the best short thesis is not operational collapse; it is multiple compression from rich valuation plus rising financing costs.

**Bottom Line**
NEE remains one of the stronger fundamental stories in the utility space, with real earnings power, strong medium-term relative performance, and clear investor confidence. But the quality comes with a premium multiple, heavy capex, rising debt, and a dividend that appears more dependent on financing flexibility than on excess free cash flow. That combination supports a constructive medium-term view only if rates stay controlled and management keeps converting large capital spending into visible earnings growth.
