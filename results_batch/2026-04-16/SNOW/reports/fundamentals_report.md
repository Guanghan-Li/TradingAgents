SNOW fundamental report based on the prefetched live fundamentals context dated April 16, 2026.

Snowflake Inc. is a large-cap application software company in the Technology sector with a market capitalization of about $49.6B. The core fundamental picture is mixed: the company is still producing strong top-line growth and very high gross margins, but GAAP profitability remains deeply negative, stock-based compensation is very large, and the stock has materially underperformed both peers and the Technology sector despite a recent 1-week bounce.

From a business quality standpoint, the strongest part of the SNOW story is revenue scale plus gross profit generation. TTM revenue is $4.68B and quarterly revenue has risen steadily over the last five reported quarters: $986.8M, $1.04B, $1.145B, $1.213B, and $1.284B in the quarter ended 2026-01-31. That implies roughly 30% year-over-year growth in the latest quarter versus 2025-01-31, which is still strong for a company already near $5B in annual revenue. Gross profit also rose from $653.6M to $857.7M over the same span, while gross margin stayed in a narrow and healthy band around 66% to 68%. That tells traders the underlying platform economics remain attractive and the core product is still scaling efficiently before operating expenses.

The main weakness is that SNOW is still far from GAAP earnings quality. TTM EPS is -3.94, TTM net income is -$1.33B, EBITDA is -$1.29B, profit margin is -28.4%, and operating margin is -33.2%. On a quarterly basis, operating income improved from -$386.7M in 2025-01-31 to -$318.2M in 2026-01-31, so there is real progress, but the latest quarter still carried a roughly -24.8% operating margin. Net loss in the latest quarter was -$309.6M, improved from -$327.5M a year earlier, and diluted EPS improved from -0.99 to -0.90 year over year. The direction is better, but the absolute level of losses is still large enough that traders should view the profitability story as an execution-dependent future outcome, not a present reality.

Operating expense intensity remains the key fundamental pressure point. In the latest quarter, operating expense was $1.176B against gross profit of $857.7M. R&D was $511.0M, SG&A was $664.8M, and selling and marketing alone was $551.3M. That spend profile shows SNOW is still paying heavily to sustain growth. Just as important, stock-based compensation was $403.6M in the latest quarter, after $412.3M and $404.2M in the two previous quarters. SBC is therefore running at roughly 31% of latest quarterly revenue, which is a major quality issue for equity holders because it props up cash flow while diluting shareholders.

Cash generation is much stronger than earnings, but traders should treat it carefully. TTM free cash flow is $1.59B, and the latest quarter produced $781.2M of operating cash flow and $763.3M of free cash flow. Capex is very light at just $17.9M in the latest quarter, so FCF conversion looks excellent. However, this is not purely a margin story. The latest quarter benefited from a $541.2M positive working-capital contribution, and current deferred revenue jumped to $3.347B from $2.424B in the prior quarter. That suggests billing timing and deferred revenue dynamics were a major driver of cash generation. For traders, this means the cash flow profile is real and valuable, but quarterly FCF should not be read as a clean proxy for recurring GAAP earnings power.

The balance sheet is solid on liquidity but weaker on equity quality. SNOW ended the latest quarter with $2.83B of cash and equivalents plus $1.20B of short-term investments, or roughly $4.03B of near-liquid resources. Total debt was $2.74B, so the company still appears to hold net cash overall and has no obvious near-term solvency problem. That said, common equity has fallen sharply to $1.92B from $3.00B a year earlier, retained earnings are deeply negative at -$9.49B, and tangible book value is only $365.2M. Debt-to-equity is elevated at 142.5, largely because equity has been compressed by accumulated losses rather than because debt has exploded. This makes SNOW financially safe in the near term, but not balance-sheet-cheap by any conventional value metric.

Shareholder dilution remains an important watch item. Diluted average shares rose from 331.4M in 2025-01-31 to 342.3M in 2026-01-31, about 3.3% growth year over year. The company has been repurchasing stock, including $150.0M in the latest quarter and $232.9M in the prior quarter, but those buybacks are only partly offsetting issuance from compensation. Additional paid-in capital rose to $11.47B, which reinforces that SBC is a central part of the capital structure. For traders, this weakens the quality of per-share compounding and should remain a core debate item around the stock.

Valuation is still demanding relative to current fundamentals. Forward PE is 59.1 and price-to-book is 25.7, even though TTM EPS is negative and return on equity is -53.9%. The market is implicitly pricing a future margin inflection and sustained high growth, not current earnings strength. That can work if SNOW proves operating leverage over the next several quarters, but it leaves little room for execution misses, especially after the sharp stock decline.

Price action confirms that investors are currently skeptical. SNOW has outperformed XLK over the last week, gaining 8.55% versus 7.00%, but the medium-term picture is much worse: -17.78% over 1 month, -30.37% over 3 months, -40.39% over 6 months, and -33.76% YTD. Relative to XLK, alpha is -26.86% over 1 month, -35.56% over 3 months, and -48.70% over 6 months. Among the listed peers, SNOW ranks last over 3 months at 9/9. That tells traders the recent bounce has not yet changed the underlying market verdict; the stock is still being repriced lower relative to the sector despite a stable macro backdrop.

Macro conditions are not the main problem here. The effective Fed Funds rate is 3.64% as of 2026-03-01, the 2Y-10Y curve is positively sloped by 0.53 points, unemployment is 4.3%, and VIX is 18.17. This is a reasonably constructive backdrop for duration-sensitive software names. Since SNOW is still underperforming badly, the current weakness likely reflects company-specific concerns about valuation, growth durability, margin credibility, or investor confidence in execution rather than broad macro stress.

Actionable trader takeaways:

- The bullish case is that SNOW still has elite software gross margins, about 30% latest-quarter revenue growth, strong liquidity, and very strong free cash flow generation. If management can keep revenue growth near current levels while pulling operating margin materially closer to breakeven, the stock can rerate sharply because the market has already compressed it.
- The bearish case is that SNOW remains a high-multiple, negative-earnings stock with heavy SBC, meaningful dilution, shrinking equity, and large operating losses. If growth slows before expense discipline improves, the current valuation can still compress further.
- The most important confirms to watch in the next reports are revenue growth durability, operating margin improvement, SBC as a percent of revenue, deferred revenue growth, and whether buybacks actually neutralize dilution.
- The strongest positive evidence in the current data is the combination of rising revenue, stable gross margins, and strong cash generation.
- The strongest negative evidence is the combination of -$1.33B TTM net income, -33.2% TTM operating margin, very high SBC, and severe relative underperformance versus peers and XLK.

Overall, SNOW fundamentals support a view of a high-quality revenue engine attached to an incomplete profitability model. This is not a broken balance sheet story, but it is also not yet a proven earnings compounder. For traders, that usually means the stock will remain highly sensitive to even small changes in growth and margin expectations.
