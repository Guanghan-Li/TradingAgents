Macro regime for `SMCI`: soft-landing / mid-cycle expansion with sticky inflation. As of March 2026, unemployment is 4.3%, payrolls rose 178k, and the Fed funds rate is 3.64% after cuts from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by January 2026, then a hold through March. At the same time, March CPI rose 0.87% sequentially and PPI rose 1.78% sequentially, so disinflation is incomplete.

The rates message is not recessionary. On April 15, 2026, the Treasury curve was positively sloped, with 2Y at 3.76% and 10Y at 4.29%, a +53 bp 2s10s spread. That usually signals normal growth expectations rather than imminent contraction. VIX at 18.17 also points to a relatively contained risk backdrop.

Policy implication: the Fed looks closer to an extended hold than an aggressive easing cycle. Growth has not broken, labor is still stable, and inflation prints are still firm enough to keep the Committee cautious. The near-term macro base case is steady nominal growth, no immediate recession signal, and persistent sensitivity to upside inflation surprises through the long end of the curve.

Cross-asset implication for `SMCI`: macro is fundamentally supportive for AI/server demand because nominal growth remains intact and recession odds look lower than they did when the curve was inverted. But it is not an ideal valuation backdrop for high-beta growth hardware, because elevated 10Y and 30Y yields can pressure multiples, especially if inflation keeps the Fed sidelined. For `SMCI`, that means macro is better for revenue demand than for multiple expansion.

Key risks the committee should incorporate for `SMCI`:
- Inflation re-acceleration keeps the Fed on hold or turns policy rhetoric hawkish again.
- Long-duration yields continue rising, compressing high-multiple tech and AI infrastructure names.
- A delayed growth slowdown hits enterprise and hyperscaler capex later than the bond market currently implies.
- If labor weakens suddenly, the curve could bull-steepen for recessionary rather than constructive reasons.
