I side with the bull analyst, but with the bear analyst’s execution discipline. My recommendation is Buy MU. The primary reason is that the structured underwriting is still net constructive: Bull 35%, Base 45%, Bear 20%, with medium-high conviction, a 2.0% initial weight, a 4.0% target weight, and a 0.5% max-loss budget. That is not a do-nothing setup. It says MU is worth owning, just not at full size on day one.

The bull’s best evidence is the magnitude and quality of the earnings cycle. MU’s latest quarter shows revenue of $23.86B versus $8.05B a year ago, EPS of $12.07 versus $1.41, operating cash flow of $11.90B, positive free cash flow of $5.52B despite $6.39B of capex, and a balance sheet with $14.59B of cash against $10.80B of debt. A 4.65 forward P/E is hard to dismiss if AI server DRAM/HBM demand and the memory pricing upcycle remain even roughly intact. The bear’s strongest points are also real: the highest-probability single scenario is Base, not Bull; MU is extended after a sharp rally; ATR of 26.68 means normal pullbacks can be violent; receivables at $17.31B need to convert; and the structured segment split is incomplete because revenue_share_pct is missing. But those arguments weaken the entry, not the thesis. The various HOLD reports are basically timing memos, while the company-level numbers still argue that MU is in a powerful earnings regime.

So the investment plan for MU is straightforward. Start with a 2.0% position now, not the full 4.0%, because I do not want to repeat the common mistake of confusing a great business setup with a perfect immediate entry. Add toward 4.0% only in two cases: either MU pulls back into roughly the 420 to 430 area and holds, or MU closes decisively above 465 to 466 with follow-through. Keep the portfolio-level max loss at 0.5%, which means position size and stop discipline matter more than prediction confidence. If MU loses the 419 area on a closing basis, reduce risk; if it breaks 404 or late-June earnings/guidance show weaker memory pricing, softer AI/HBM demand, or gross-margin rollover, reassess the long thesis aggressively. No past mistake reflections were provided, so the adjustment I am making explicitly is to avoid hiding in Hold when the evidence is net positive, while also avoiding the equally common mistake of chasing MU at full size into resistance. Netting it out, MU is a Buy, not a Hold, because the strongest evidence says the company deserves exposure now and the bear case is mainly telling you how to size and time that exposure, not why to avoid it.
