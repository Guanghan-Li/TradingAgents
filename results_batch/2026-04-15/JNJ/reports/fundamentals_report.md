**JNJ Fundamental Report**

Johnson & Johnson (`JNJ`) remains a high-quality, defensive healthcare mega-cap with strong profitability, strong free-cash-flow generation, low share-price volatility, and clear recent relative strength. In the provided live dataset as of **April 15, 2026**, the key fundamental question is not whether the core business is profitable, because it clearly is, but whether traders should view the current premium as justified given rising leverage, heavy acquisition activity, and an increasingly intangible-heavy balance sheet.

No new financial statement was provided within the last week in this dataset; the latest reported quarter is **December 31, 2025**. Over the **1-week** period into **April 15, 2026**, `JNJ` returned **-1.09%**, which was slightly better than the healthcare ETF `XLV` at **-1.27%**. That matters because the recent pullback has happened after very strong intermediate-term outperformance, not during fundamental collapse.

**Company Profile And Current Setup**

`JNJ` is classified here as a **Healthcare** company in **Drug Manufacturers - General**. It is operating at very large scale, with a market capitalization of about **$575.2 billion**. The stock still screens as a classic defensive large-cap profile:

- `Beta`: **0.329**, indicating materially lower volatility than the broader market.
- `Dividend yield`: **2.23%**, which remains meaningful in a 3.6% Fed-funds environment.
- `TTM revenue`: **$96.36 billion**.
- `TTM EBITDA`: **$34.10 billion**.
- `TTM net income`: **$21.04 billion**.
- `Operating margin`: **26.72%**.
- `Profit margin`: **21.83%**.

Valuation is not cheap in absolute terms, but it is not extreme for a defensive quality compounder:

- `P/E (TTM)`: **21.66x**.
- `Forward P/E`: **18.83x**.
- `EPS (TTM)`: **11.02**.
- `Forward EPS`: **12.68**.

The drop from trailing to forward P/E implies the market is expecting continued EPS growth and/or cleaner earnings composition ahead. A rough read-through is that the market is willing to pay a premium for stability, cash generation, and defensive healthcare exposure.

**Income Statement Analysis**

The core revenue trend improved through 2025. Quarterly revenue moved from **$21.893 billion** on **March 31, 2025** to **$23.743 billion** on **June 30, 2025**, **$23.993 billion** on **September 30, 2025**, and **$24.564 billion** on **December 31, 2025**. That is a steady sequential build, and the latest quarter was also above the **$22.520 billion** reported on **December 31, 2024**.

The latest quarter looked fundamentally solid:

- `Q4 2025 revenue`: **$24.564 billion**.
- `Q4 2025 gross profit`: **$16.596 billion**.
- `Q4 2025 operating income`: **$5.591 billion**.
- `Q4 2025 net income`: **$5.116 billion**.
- `Q4 2025 diluted EPS`: **2.10**.

That quarter suggests healthy operating momentum. Gross margin in `Q4 2025` was about **67.6%** (`16.596 / 24.564`), which is consistent with a very strong branded-healthcare earnings model.

There is, however, an important earnings-quality caveat. The quarter ended **March 31, 2025** included **$6.883 billion** of total unusual items, driving reported net income to **$10.999 billion** and diluted EPS to **4.54**. That result is not representative of normalized recurring earnings power. The data itself shows this clearly:

- `Q1 2025 reported net income`: **$10.999 billion**.
- `Q1 2025 normalized income`: **$5.444 billion**.

That gap is too large to ignore. Traders should therefore place more weight on the normalized cadence of roughly **$5.0 billion to $5.9 billion** in quarterly income rather than on the headline spike from `Q1 2025`.

A few additional operating observations:

- `R&D` remains elevated but manageable. It was **$4.252 billion** in `Q4 2025`, after **$3.672 billion** in `Q3 2025` and **$5.298 billion** in `Q4 2024`.
- `SG&A` was **$6.753 billion** in `Q4 2025`, up modestly from **$6.453 billion** in `Q4 2024`.
- `Normalized EBITDA` in recent quarters stayed high, ranging from **$7.956 billion** to **$9.278 billion** across the last three reported quarters of 2025.

Bottom line on earnings: the operating engine looks healthy, but reported EPS has been noisy because of one-time items. For trading purposes, `JNJ` currently looks stronger when judged on revenue growth, margins, and normalized earnings than when judged on raw GAAP quarter-to-quarter EPS swings.

**Balance Sheet Analysis**

The balance sheet is still investment-grade in character, but it is meaningfully more leveraged and more acquisition-heavy than the headline quality image alone would suggest.

As of **December 31, 2025**:

- `Total assets`: **$199.21 billion**.
- `Total liabilities`: **$117.67 billion**.
- `Stockholders' equity`: **$81.54 billion**.
- `Cash and equivalents + short-term investments`: **$20.10 billion**.
- `Total debt`: **$47.93 billion**.
- `Net debt`: **$28.22 billion**.
- `Working capital`: **$1.50 billion**.

Key changes versus **December 31, 2024** are notable:

- `Total debt` increased from **$36.63 billion** to **$47.93 billion**.
- `Net debt` increased from **$12.53 billion** to **$28.22 billion**.
- `Cash and short-term investments` fell from **$24.52 billion** to **$20.10 billion**.
- `Long-term debt` rose from **$30.65 billion** to **$39.44 billion**.

This is not a distressed balance sheet, but it is a meaningful leverage step-up.

The most important structural issue is the asset mix. `Goodwill and other intangible assets` were **$99.18 billion** at **December 31, 2025**, which is roughly half of total assets and greater than total tangible equity. `Tangible book value` was **negative $17.63 billion**. That does not create an immediate operating problem, but it does mean:

- book value is less informative than cash flow for valuation,
- acquisition execution matters a lot,
- impairment risk is a real medium-term watch item if acquired assets underperform.

Liquidity is adequate but not loose. `Current assets` were **$55.62 billion** against `current liabilities` of **$54.13 billion`, for a current ratio just above **1.0x**. That is workable for a company of this scale, but it does not leave a large short-term liquidity cushion.

Working-capital trends also tightened during 2025. `Working capital` fell from **$5.57 billion** on **December 31, 2024** to **$1.50 billion** on **December 31, 2025**. Inventory rose from **$12.44 billion** to **$14.19 billion**, and receivables rose from **$18.93 billion** to **$21.33 billion** over the same period. Those are not alarming by themselves, but they help explain why cash conversion was uneven through the year.

**Cash Flow And Capital Allocation**

This is still one of `JNJ`'s strongest fundamental pillars. Even with acquisition spending and leverage changes, the core business is producing substantial operating and free cash flow.

Quarterly cash flow for **December 31, 2025**:

- `Operating cash flow`: **$7.309 billion**.
- `Capital expenditure`: **$1.837 billion**.
- `Free cash flow`: **$5.472 billion**.
- `Dividends paid`: **$3.131 billion**.
- `Share repurchases`: **$1.924 billion**.

That means `Q4 2025` free cash flow covered the dividend comfortably and covered dividend plus buybacks only narrowly. The latest quarter still supports the shareholder-return model, but it leaves less room for error than the business historically had.

Using the four reported quarters of 2025 in the provided dataset:

- `Total operating cash flow`: about **$24.53 billion**.
- `Total capex`: about **$5.22 billion**.
- `Total free cash flow`: about **$19.31 billion**.
- `Total dividends paid`: about **$12.38 billion**.
- `Total buybacks`: about **$5.95 billion**.

That implies free cash flow broadly covered shareholder distributions during 2025, which is a positive sign. However, acquisition spending was very large:

- `Net business purchase and sale` was **-$14.458 billion** in `Q2 2025`.
- `Net business purchase and sale` was **-$3.082 billion** in `Q4 2025`.

Those outlays are the clearest reason debt rose and the balance sheet became more intangible-heavy. This is the central fundamental trade-off in the report:

- core operations remain strong and cash generative,
- but capital allocation has shifted toward external growth and balance-sheet expansion.

For traders, that means future execution on acquired assets matters more than usual. If integration is smooth, the business can support the leverage. If integration disappoints, the balance-sheet quality debate becomes much louder.

- `10-year Treasury`: **4.26%** as of **April 14, 2026**.

- A normal yield curve reduces recession stress signals, which helps risk appetite broadly.

The main macro risk is not financing stress today. It is valuation sensitivity if bond yields move higher while earnings growth cools.

**Actionable Fundamental Insights For Traders**

1. The core business is stronger than the past week's price action suggests. Revenue, gross profit, and normalized earnings all support a constructive view on operating fundamentals.

2. Earnings quality needs normalization. The quarter ended **March 31, 2025** had a large one-time benefit, so traders should avoid over-annualizing headline EPS.

3. Free cash flow remains a major support pillar. `JNJ` still funds its dividend and most capital returns from operations, which limits downside in a risk-off tape.

4. Balance-sheet quality has weakened at the margin. Rising debt, lower cash, thinner working capital, and very large goodwill/intangibles mean the stock is less pristine than the brand reputation alone implies.

5. Price-to-book is not the right anchor. With `P/B` at **7.05x** and negative tangible book value, valuation work should focus more on earnings durability, free cash flow, and forward EPS rather than accounting book value.

6. Relative strength is already strong. Because `JNJ` has outperformed both peers and `XLV` over 3 months, upside may now require either additional positive execution or a continued defensive rotation rather than simple mean reversion.

7. The best near-term confirmation signal would be another quarter showing revenue above roughly **$24 billion**, normalized EPS in the low-`$2` range or better, and free cash flow comfortably above the dividend. That would validate the recent rerating.

8. The biggest fundamental watchpoints are acquisition integration, debt stabilization, working-capital normalization, and whether intangible-heavy assets translate into sustained earnings growth rather than future write-down risk.

**Fundamental Bottom Line**

`JNJ` looks fundamentally solid to strong on operations, cash generation, and defensive market positioning. The business has scale, durable margins, a low-beta profile, and enough free cash flow to keep supporting dividends and shareholder returns. The caution is that recent balance-sheet changes make the story less purely defensive than it appears at first glance. If traders are constructive, the strongest part of the thesis is operating resilience and cash flow. If traders are cautious, the strongest pushback is that the stock has already rerated meaningfully while leverage and intangible exposure have risen.
