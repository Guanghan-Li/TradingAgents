Macro regime for `GLD`: late-cycle but not recessionary. Growth and labor remain resilient enough to keep the Fed from easing aggressively, while inflation looks sticky to re-accelerating at the margin. That combination argues for a higher-for-longer policy pause rather than a near-term dovish pivot.

Policy backdrop: the effective Fed funds rate has fallen from 4.09% in October 2025 to 3.64% by January 2026, then held flat through March 2026. The Treasury curve is now positively sloped, with 2Y at 3.76% and 10Y at 4.26% for a `+50 bp` 2s10s spread, which removes the classic recession/inversion tailwind that often helps defensive macro trades. Long-end yields near 4.8%-4.9% suggest term premium and inflation risk are still being priced.

Implications for `GLD`: this is a mixed macro setup. Supportive inputs are sticky inflation, a Fed that may be reluctant to declare victory, and elevated long-duration rate risk that can sustain demand for hard-asset hedges. Headwinds are the absence of a clear recession signal, stable labor data, subdued volatility, and the possibility that real yields stay firm if nominal yields remain elevated without a growth collapse. Net: macro is `neutral to mildly supportive` for `GLD` over an intermediate horizon, but not a clean bullish impulse unless real yields roll over or the Fed turns more explicitly dovish.

Key risks other analysts should incorporate: a hotter inflation sequence that pushes real yields higher would pressure `GLD`; a sudden growth break or risk-off shock would improve the gold case; persistent curve steepening driven by inflation/term premium is more supportive than steepening driven by strong real growth.
