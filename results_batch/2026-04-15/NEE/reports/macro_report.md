Macro view for `NEE`:

`NEE` is operating in a soft-landing / disinflationary-but-not-deflationary macro regime. Growth still looks positive, labor is holding up, and the Treasury curve has re-steepened into a normal upward slope. The key message from the prefetched data is that the Fed has already eased materially from late 2025 into early 2026, then paused around 3.64%, while inflation has not fully disappeared. That combination argues for a base case of policy on hold in the near term with an easing bias only if inflation cools further or labor weakens.

For `NEE`, that backdrop is mixed but broadly better than the 2024-2025 higher-rate regime. Utilities and long-duration infrastructure cash flows usually benefit when the market believes peak policy restraint is over. The positive 2Y-10Y spread of +0.50% says the bond market is no longer pricing an imminent recession, which reduces tail-risk to power demand and financing access, but the still-high 10Y yield of 4.26% and 30Y yield of 4.87% mean long-duration equity valuation pressure has not fully gone away. In practical terms: the Fed is less of a headwind for `NEE` than it was, but the long end of the curve is still expensive enough to cap multiple expansion.

Inflation is the main macro complication. CPI and PPI both accelerated sequentially in March 2026, with PPI especially firm. If that firmness persists, the Fed may stay paused longer than rate-sensitive equities want, and long yields could remain elevated or move higher. That is not ideal for `NEE`, because utilities, renewables development, and capital-intensive grid investment stories are sensitive to discount rates and funding costs. A higher-for-longer long bond market matters more for `NEE` than the unchanged front-end policy rate alone.

Labor data still looks resilient rather than recessionary: unemployment edged down to 4.3% and payrolls increased by 178k. That supports a constructive macro demand backdrop and lowers near-term recession odds, but it also means the Fed has less urgency to cut. For `NEE`, that is a classic tradeoff: better macro stability and credit conditions, but less immediate relief from rates.

Cross-asset implications for `NEE`:

- Rates: the biggest macro driver remains the 10Y-30Y Treasury complex, not just Fed funds. If long yields drift lower, `NEE` should benefit disproportionately versus more cyclical equities.
- Credit: a normal curve and contained VIX around 18 suggest financing markets are functioning normally, which is supportive for capital-intensive utilities and renewable buildout.
- Equity style: this regime should favor quality defensives when growth moderates but does not break. `NEE` fits that better than deep cyclicals.
- Inflation hedge rotation: if PPI/CPI remain sticky, markets may rotate away from duration-sensitive defensives, which would be a relative headwind for `NEE`.

Likely policy direction: near-term Fed hold, medium-term easing bias, but only conditional on inflation resuming a cooler trend. The macro base case is not recession, not overheating, but late-cycle normalization with sticky pockets of inflation. That is mildly constructive for `NEE` relative to a renewed hiking cycle, but not as supportive as a clean bond rally would be.

Key macro risks other analysts should incorporate for `NEE`:

- Re-acceleration in inflation keeps the Fed sidelined and pushes 10Y/30Y yields higher.
- Long-end term premium rises even if Fed funds stays unchanged, pressuring utility valuations.
- Strong labor and steady growth delay cuts, extending the higher-real-rate environment.
- If soft-landing confidence improves too much, investors may prefer cyclicals over defensives, limiting relative upside for `NEE`.
- If growth unexpectedly rolls over, `NEE` may outperform cyclicals defensively, but financing and project sentiment could still tighten if credit spreads widen.

Overall macro stance for `NEE`: modestly constructive but rate-dependent. The macro regime is better than a tightening cycle, yet still not cleanly bullish because long-duration yields remain elevated and inflation has shown fresh firmness.
