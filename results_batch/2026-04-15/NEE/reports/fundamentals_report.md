## NEE Fundamental Report

NEE remains a high-quality utility and power infrastructure name with a premium market multiple, strong medium-term earnings momentum, and clear evidence of continued asset growth, but the balance sheet and funding profile are the main constraints. The fundamental picture is positive on franchise quality and recent earnings, mixed on cash generation after capital spending, and sensitive to interest rates because NEE is still financing a very large buildout. For traders, the setup looks more like a quality compounder in a consolidation phase than a clean deep-value entry.

## Company Profile and Core Fundamental Identity

NEE is a regulated electric utility and clean energy operator. The core investment case is the combination of relatively stable regulated utility earnings with a large, capital-intensive renewable and infrastructure growth pipeline. That combination usually deserves a better multiple than a slow-growth utility, but it also makes NEE more dependent on debt markets and equity market access than a simpler regulated utility peer.

The market is still assigning NEE a premium profile:

- Market cap is about $190.3 billion.
- TTM P/E is 27.65 and forward P/E is 20.87.
- Price to book is 3.48.
- Dividend yield is 2.73%.
- Beta is 0.733, which is consistent with a defensive utility profile.

That valuation says investors are paying up for growth visibility and perceived asset quality. It also means NEE has less room for operational disappointment than a cheaper regulated utility.

- Net income common stockholders was $1.535 billion versus $1.203 billion a year earlier, up about 27.6%.

A useful summary is that NEE is generating real earnings growth, but not all reported upside is purely from simple base-rate utility operations. The business is performing well, but investors should prefer normalized earnings trends over raw GAAP prints.

## Balance Sheet: Growth Is Real, Leverage Is Also Real

The balance sheet shows exactly what the NEE story usually looks like: significant asset growth funded with a mix of rising debt, some equity issuance, and internally generated cash.

From 2024-12-31 to 2025-12-31:

- Total assets rose from $190.1 billion to $212.7 billion, up about $22.6 billion.
- Net PPE rose from $138.9 billion to $156.2 billion, up about $17.3 billion.
- Construction in progress increased from $21.7 billion to $24.6 billion.
- Stockholders' equity rose from $50.1 billion to $54.6 billion.
- Total debt rose from $82.3 billion to $95.6 billion, up about $13.3 billion.
- Net debt rose from $80.8 billion to $92.8 billion.

That is an unmistakable expansion profile. NEE is still building. The asset base is moving in the right direction if management can earn attractive returns on that capital, but the financing side matters because leverage is not low.

Key balance-sheet pressure points:

- Debt to equity is 146.24, high for any business and meaningful even for a capital-heavy utility.
- Current ratio is 0.595.
- Working capital was negative $9.23 billion at 2025-12-31.
- Long-term debt alone was $89.56 billion.

There is one constructive detail inside the liability mix: current debt fell from $9.95 billion at 2024-12-31 to $6.06 billion at 2025-12-31, while long-term debt increased. That suggests NEE pushed some obligations further out the curve rather than letting near-term maturity pressure build. Even so, the company remains dependent on continued access to reasonably priced capital.

For traders, the correct interpretation is not simply “high debt bad.” It is more specific: NEE can carry leverage because of the stability of utility cash flows, but the equity multiple will remain highly sensitive to long-duration rate expectations and any signs that project returns are compressing.

## Cash Flow, Capex, and Dividend Coverage

This is the most important area to watch.

Quarterly cash flow for 2025 was healthy before capex:

- Operating cash flow for the four 2025 quarters totaled about $12.49 billion.
- Capital expenditures for the four 2025 quarters totaled about $9.27 billion.
- Reported quarterly free cash flow for those four quarters totaled about $3.21 billion.

That means NEE did generate positive post-capex cash flow across 2025 on the quarterly statement series. However, the live fundamentals snapshot also reports trailing free cash flow of negative $15.21 billion. That does not reconcile cleanly with the quarterly figures provided here, and the same issue appears in other headline items such as EBITDA. Traders should treat the quarterly statement series as more useful than a single vendor headline number and recognize that there may be a definition mismatch in the feed.

Dividend and financing implications are clearer:

- Common dividends paid in 2025 totaled about $4.68 billion.
- Reported 2025 quarterly free cash flow of about $3.21 billion did not fully cover those dividends.
- Financing cash flow was positive in every 2025 quarter.
- Net issuance of debt was material throughout the year.
- NEE also issued about $2.006 billion of equity in the 2025-09-30 quarter.

This is the core funding story: NEE is not in distress, but it is not self-funding all of its growth and shareholder returns from post-capex free cash flow either. It is using the balance sheet and, at times, equity issuance to support the model. That is workable when capital markets are cooperative. It becomes a valuation risk when long-end yields rise or the market becomes less willing to fund utility growth at premium multiples.

The current macro environment is not hostile, but it is not ideal for a highly capital-intensive utility either.

- Effective Fed funds rate is 3.64% as of 2026-03-01.
- The Treasury curve is upward sloping, with the 10-year at 4.26% and 30-year at 4.87% as of 2026-04-14.
- CPI and PPI both moved higher sequentially in the latest reading window.
- VIX at 18.36 is not signaling panic.

For NEE, the most relevant macro fact is not equity volatility; it is the cost of long-duration capital. The company’s business model works best when investors are comfortable underwriting long-lived infrastructure cash flows and when financing costs do not erode project economics. A 4% to 5% long-end yield environment is manageable, but it limits multiple expansion and raises the bar for incremental returns on new investment.

## Trader-Focused Fundamental Takeaways

1. NEE’s latest quarter was fundamentally strong. Revenue, EPS, and net income all improved materially year over year, and normalized earnings still looked solid.

2. The balance sheet is the key constraint. Debt is high, working capital is negative, and the company is clearly still in capital deployment mode.

3. The dividend appears supported by business stability, but not fully covered by post-capex free cash flow on the quarterly series. That makes funding conditions important.

4. The stock already trades at a premium. Forward growth expectations are doing a lot of work in the valuation.

5. The last week’s price weakness is not yet a fundamental red flag. NEE has still outperformed most peers and the utility ETF over 3 months, 6 months, and YTD.

6. Data-quality mismatches in the live feed matter. The reported TTM free cash flow and EBITDA do not reconcile cleanly with the quarterly statements shown here. Traders should lean more heavily on the statement trend than on a single headline field.

## Practical Trading Interpretation for NEE

The fundamental case supports a **HOLD** rather than an aggressive BUY or SELL.

Why not BUY:

- Valuation is already elevated for a utility.
- Debt and external funding dependence limit upside if rates remain sticky.
- Dividend coverage after capex is not clean enough to justify a pure income-premium argument.

Why not SELL:

- Operating momentum is still good.
- Asset growth is real, not just narrative.
- Relative performance remains strong over medium horizons.
- The business mix is higher quality than many plain regulated peers.

The most actionable setup for traders is to watch whether NEE can defend premium valuation while rates stay firm. If long-end yields ease or if another quarter confirms earnings growth without further balance-sheet deterioration, the bullish case strengthens. If yields rise again or capital needs expand faster than earnings and cash flow, the premium multiple is vulnerable.
