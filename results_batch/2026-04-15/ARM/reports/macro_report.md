Macro view for `ARM`: the backdrop is a soft-landing / late-cycle expansion, not a recession regime. Labor is still holding up (`4.3%` unemployment, `+178k` payrolls), the Treasury curve is positively sloped (`2Y-10Y +50 bps`), and VIX is relatively contained (`18.36`). That is supportive for risk appetite and end-demand expectations tied to semis and compute infrastructure.

The complication for `ARM` is inflation and rates. March CPI and PPI both re-accelerated on the latest print, while the Fed has already eased materially from late 2025 and is now paused around `3.64%`. That combination points to a "higher-for-longer near neutral" policy regime rather than an imminent new easing cycle. The curve shape also looks more like normal growth plus term-premium pressure than recession pricing. For `ARM`, that means the macro backdrop is fundamentally better for revenues than for valuation multiples: growth-sensitive tech can keep benefiting from stable activity, but long-duration semiconductor names remain exposed to higher long-end yields, especially with the 10Y at `4.26%` and 30Y near `4.87%`.

Policy path: the Fed likely stays on hold unless inflation cools again or labor weakens materially. The risk balance for `ARM` is therefore two-sided: stable growth supports semiconductor demand, but any renewed inflation surprise could push real yields higher and pressure premium tech multiples. Other analysts should incorporate a regime of decent nominal growth, sticky inflation, paused Fed, and valuation sensitivity to rates rather than a clean liquidity tailwind.

Cross-asset implications for `ARM`:
- Equities: supportive macro growth backdrop, but leadership in long-duration tech is vulnerable to rate back-ups.
- Rates: bear-steepening / elevated term premium is the main macro headwind for `ARM` multiple expansion.
- Credit: still consistent with manageable stress, which helps cyclical tech demand.
- USD/liquidity: a paused Fed with firm long yields can restrain the breadth of risk-on moves.

Key risks for `ARM`:
- Inflation re-acceleration forces markets to price fewer cuts or even renewed hawkishness.
- Long-end yields keep rising even without Fed hikes, compressing semiconductor valuation multiples.
- Labor softens abruptly, undermining the current soft-landing interpretation.
- Growth stays okay, but leadership rotates away from duration-heavy tech into cyclicals/value.
