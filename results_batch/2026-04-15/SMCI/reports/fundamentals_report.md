SMCI fundamental report as of 2026-04-15

SMCI is a Technology / Computer Hardware company with a market capitalization of about $16.39 billion. On the surface, valuation looks compressed versus its own growth profile: trailing P/E is 19.92x, forward P/E is 9.07x, price-to-book is 2.34x, trailing EPS is $1.37, and forward EPS is $3.01. That setup implies the market expects a material earnings recovery but is still applying a discount because execution risk remains high.

From a business-quality standpoint, SMCI is still producing scale. TTM revenue is $28.06 billion, EBITDA is $1.10 billion, and net income is $872.8 million. Profitability, however, remains thin for a hardware name with major working-capital intensity: gross profit is $2.25 billion, gross margin is roughly 8.0% on TTM revenue, operating margin is 3.74%, and net margin is 3.11%. Return metrics are acceptable but not exceptional given volatility: ROE is 13.19% and ROA is 3.42%.

The most important recent fundamental development for SMCI is the sharp acceleration in the latest reported quarter ended 2025-12-31. Revenue reached $12.68 billion versus $5.02 billion in 2025-09-30 and $5.68 billion in 2024-12-31. Gross profit rose to $798.6 million from $467.4 million in the prior quarter and $670.0 million a year earlier. Operating income increased to $474.3 million, and net income climbed to $400.6 million. Diluted EPS was $0.60 versus $0.26 in the immediately prior quarter and $0.51 a year earlier. On headline income statement performance, SMCI clearly reaccelerated.

That said, the quality of the quarter needs careful interpretation because the balance sheet and cash flow statement moved dramatically at the same time. Total assets nearly doubled sequentially to $28.00 billion from $14.39 billion. Current assets rose to $26.12 billion from $12.66 billion. The largest drivers were accounts receivable jumping to $11.00 billion from $2.53 billion, inventory increasing to $10.60 billion from $5.73 billion, and cash remaining high at $4.09 billion. On the liability side, current liabilities surged to $15.40 billion from $2.35 billion, with payables and accrued expenses reaching $14.27 billion and accounts payable alone hitting $13.75 billion. This is the central balance-sheet issue in SMCI right now: the business is scaling fast, but working capital has become extremely large and operationally sensitive.

Cash flow confirms that point. In the quarter ended 2025-12-31, operating cash flow was negative $24.0 million and free cash flow was negative $45.1 million despite net income of $400.6 million. The reason was working-capital distortion. Receivables increased by $8.47 billion and inventory increased by $5.00 billion, partially offset by a $12.47 billion rise in payables and other accrual movements. This means reported earnings were strong, but cash conversion was weak. Traders should treat this as a major watch item rather than a minor accounting footnote.

Looking over the last five reported quarters, SMCI’s earnings profile has been volatile but directionally positive in the latest print:

- 2025-12-31: revenue $12.68B, operating income $474.3M, net income $400.6M, diluted EPS $0.60
- 2025-09-30: revenue $5.02B, operating income $182.3M, net income $168.3M, diluted EPS $0.26
- 2025-06-30: revenue $5.76B, operating income $228.4M, net income $195.2M, diluted EPS $0.31
- 2025-03-31: revenue $4.60B, operating income $146.8M, net income $108.8M, diluted EPS $0.17
- 2024-12-31: revenue $5.68B, operating income $368.6M, net income $320.6M, diluted EPS $0.51

This shows two things at once. First, SMCI still has the capacity to generate very large revenue bursts when demand and fulfillment align. Second, quarter-to-quarter consistency is not yet strong enough to justify a clean premium multiple without skepticism.

Capital structure has also changed materially. Total debt rose to $4.91 billion as of 2025-12-31 from $1.92 billion a year earlier, while net debt was $786.8 million. Debt-to-equity is 75.28, which is elevated for a company with thin margins and unstable working-capital swings. Liquidity is not immediately distressed because the current ratio is 1.70 and cash exceeds $4.0 billion, but leverage has clearly increased. Equity also improved to $6.99 billion, and tangible book value reached $6.99 billion, so the company is not balance-sheet impaired; it is balance-sheet stretched by growth.

Operationally, SMCI appears to be in a scale-up phase that is consuming balance-sheet capacity. Inventory rose from $3.60 billion at 2024-12-31 to $10.60 billion at 2025-12-31. Receivables rose from $3.06 billion to $11.00 billion over the same period. Accounts payable rose from $549.3 million to $13.75 billion. Those are not normal incremental changes; they indicate aggressive buildout, fulfillment timing, supplier financing, or a combination of all three. For traders, this is the key fundamental debate in SMCI: is the company temporarily warehousing growth ahead of collections, or is it absorbing mounting execution risk in a low-margin business?

There are also a few positive signs beneath the noise. Retained earnings increased to $4.00 billion from $3.33 billion a year earlier. Cash remains substantial. Gross profit in the latest quarter was the highest in the provided series. Normalized EBITDA reached $548.0 million in 2025-12-31, more than double the 2025-09-30 level. Forward EPS of $3.01 versus trailing EPS of $1.37 suggests consensus or forward modeling expects a much stronger earnings run-rate ahead. If SMCI can normalize cash conversion and keep margins stable, current valuation could look inexpensive.

Market behavior suggests investors are still not fully convinced. SMCI outperformed strongly over the last week, up 16.77% versus XLK at 6.08%, producing 10.70 points of weekly alpha. But that rebound sits on top of weak medium-term performance: -14.34% over one month, -4.58% over three months, -50.16% over six months, and -11.85% YTD. Relative to the Technology sector ETF XLK, alpha is deeply negative over 1-month, 3-month, 6-month, and YTD windows. In other words, the market is willing to trade sharp tactical rebounds in SMCI, but the broader rerating remains unresolved.

Macro backdrop is mixed but not obviously hostile for SMCI. CPI rose 0.87% sequentially in the latest read and PPI rose 1.78%, so inflation pressure is still present. Unemployment at 4.30% is not recessionary. The Treasury curve shows short rates around 3.7% and long yields near 4.8%-4.9%, which means financing is not cheap. For a company like SMCI that relies on working capital and has raised debt materially, a higher-for-longer rate structure matters. It does not break the story, but it raises the penalty for operational mistakes.

Actionable takeaways for traders:

1. The bullish case on SMCI is earnings acceleration plus multiple compression. Revenue nearly doubled sequentially in the latest quarter, net income more than doubled versus the prior quarter, and forward P/E of 9.07x is low if the new earnings level proves durable.
2. The main bear case is cash quality and balance-sheet stress. Negative free cash flow in the latest quarter despite strong earnings, together with massive increases in receivables, inventory, and payables, means the latest print cannot be judged on EPS alone.
3. The next key confirmation point is working-capital normalization. Traders should watch whether receivables growth slows, inventory stabilizes, and operating cash flow turns sustainably positive in the next report.
4. Debt has become important. SMCI still has liquidity, but the increase in total debt to $4.91 billion reduces room for execution errors.
5. The stock is behaving like a high-beta recovery trade, not a fully trusted compounder. Beta is 1.629, and the return profile shows sharp rebounds inside a still-damaged medium-term trend.

Bottom line: SMCI’s fundamentals show a company with real scale, renewed top-line momentum, and seemingly cheap forward valuation, but also unusually aggressive working-capital expansion and weaker cash realization than the income statement suggests. For traders, SMCI is fundamentally interesting because upside is substantial if the latest revenue surge converts cleanly into cash and normalized margins, but the current statements still require caution because balance-sheet quality has become the primary swing factor.
