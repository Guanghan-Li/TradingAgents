Macro view for `O`: the backdrop is a soft-landing / late-cycle regime, but it is not a clean easing regime. Labor remains firm (`4.3%` unemployment, `+178k` payrolls), the curve is positively sloped (`2Y-10Y +50 bps`), and VIX is subdued, which argues against an imminent recession. The problem is inflation reacceleration: March CPI and PPI both stepped up materially on a sequential basis, while the Fed has already cut from `4.09%` in October 2025 to `3.64%` by March 2026 and is now paused.

That combination points to a likely Fed path of `hold` with a hawkish bias rather than renewed near-term cuts. For `O`, that is mixed to negative. Stable growth and labor support tenant health, occupancy, and rent collection, but a higher long end (`10Y 4.26%`, `30Y 4.87%`) keeps pressure on REIT valuation multiples, acquisition spreads, and external financing costs. In other words, the macro tape supports `O` fundamentals more than it supports `O` multiple expansion.

Cross-asset implication: this looks more like a carry/selectivity environment than a duration-beta rally. If long yields stay elevated or steepen further, `O` can lag even without a growth scare. `O` improves macro-wise if inflation rolls back over and the 10Y declines; `O` worsens if inflation stays sticky enough to keep real rates and term premium high.

Key risks other analysts should incorporate for `O`:
- Sticky inflation forcing a longer Fed pause or renewed hawkishness.
- Bear steepening in Treasuries lifting REIT discount rates.
- A delayed growth slowdown that hurts cyclical assets later without delivering the lower yields REITs would want today.
- Funding-market sensitivity: `O` is more exposed to long-duration financing conditions than many non-rate-sensitive equities.
