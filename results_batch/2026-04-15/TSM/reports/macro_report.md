Macro view for TSM: the U.S. macro regime is best characterized as late-cycle stabilization with sticky inflation risk. Growth and labor are not flashing recession: unemployment is 4.30%, down 0.10 percentage points sequentially, and nonfarm payrolls rose by 178k in March 2026. However, inflation momentum is uncomfortable, with CPI up 0.87% sequentially and PPI up 1.78%, which limits the Fed’s ability to accelerate easing.

The Fed backdrop is neutral-to-slightly accommodative compared with late-2025 levels, but not clearly dovish. Effective Fed Funds is 3.64%, unchanged since February 2026 after cuts from 4.09% in October 2025. The likely near-term policy path is a pause unless inflation cools. That matters for TSM because long-duration growth and semiconductor equities typically benefit from lower real rates, but sticky inflation can keep discount rates elevated and cap valuation expansion.

The Treasury curve is no longer sending a classic front-end inversion recession signal, with 1M, 3M, and 1Y all near 3.71%, 2Y at 3.76%, and 5Y at 3.87%. The long end is materially higher, with the 30Y at 4.87%, creating a steep 2Y/30Y spread of roughly +111 bps. This is more consistent with term-premium, fiscal, or inflation-risk pricing than imminent recession. For TSM, that mix is constructive for demand if global growth holds, but negative for valuation if long yields keep rising.

Cross-asset implications for TSM: a stable labor market and non-recessionary curve support semiconductor demand expectations, AI capex durability, and cyclical risk appetite. The key offset is that hot CPI/PPI prints and a steep long end may restrain multiple expansion, especially for high-quality tech names already sensitive to rates. Macro therefore supports owning TSM selectively, but not aggressively adding without confirmation that inflation and long yields are easing.

Key risks for TSM from the macro setup are: renewed inflation pressure forcing the Fed to stay restrictive longer; further bear-steepening in the long end pressuring equity multiples; U.S. dollar strength tightening global financial conditions; and any growth slowdown that turns current labor resilience into weakening end-demand for electronics, cloud capex, or industrial chips.
