There are three likely reasons.

First, **China matters disproportionately at the margin**. Even if ASML remains globally dominant, a hit to China can still change how investors model growth, backlog conversion, and margin mix.

Second, **guidance upgrades were probably not enough to erase concerns about quality of demand**. Markets care not just about whether demand exists, but where it comes from and how durable it is. AI demand is a powerful offset, but if investors think some of that upside is concentrated or already well understood, they may still punish any worsening in China.

Third, **expectations were likely high into earnings**. Barron’s framing is especially useful here: a beat that fails to sustain a rally often means the stock needed more than “better than expected.” It needed a cleaner story.

For a premium semiconductor equipment name like **ASML**, investors usually want some combination of:

- strong results,
- higher guidance,
- clean regional commentary,
- no worsening regulatory risk.

This week, they got the first two but not the last two.
