MO fundamental report based on the prefetched live fundamentals context dated 2026-04-15.

MO is Altria Group, Inc., a large-cap Consumer Defensive tobacco company with a market capitalization of about $107.7 billion. From a fundamental perspective, MO remains a classic cash-generation and capital-return story: high margins, low capital intensity, strong free cash flow, and a large dividend. The key constraints are equally clear: negative book equity, elevated leverage, weak current liquidity, and ongoing impairment/special-charge noise tied to asset valuations and portfolio repositioning. For traders, that means MO screens as a defensively positioned, income-heavy equity with resilient operating economics, but not a balance-sheet-clean compounder.

**Company Profile And Business Quality**

MO operates in the tobacco industry, which typically produces strong pricing power, low capital expenditure needs, and durable cash conversion despite low volume-growth expectations. The reported fundamentals fit that pattern closely:

- TTM revenue is about $20.14 billion.
- Gross profit is about $17.59 billion, implying an extremely strong gross margin near 87%.
- EBITDA is about $15.61 billion, implying EBITDA margin near 77%.
- Net income is about $6.93 billion, with net margin near 34.5%.
- Free cash flow is about $9.23 billion.
- Beta is 0.447, indicating lower market sensitivity than the broader equity market.

These are unusually strong profitability and cash-generation characteristics for a company of this size. Even allowing for accounting noise, MO’s underlying business appears highly efficient. For traders, that matters because it supports downside resilience during risk-off periods and helps sustain the dividend even if revenue growth remains modest.

**Balance Sheet And Capital Structure**

MO’s balance sheet is the main area of caution.

- Total debt: $25.709 billion
- Net debt: $21.235 billion

This is not a conservative balance sheet. Negative equity and negative working capital are long-standing features of MO’s capital structure, driven by debt, buybacks, and large shareholder distributions. That structure is sustainable only as long as cash flow remains strong and debt markets remain accessible.

There are some offsets:

- Cash increased to $4.474 billion at 2025-12-31 from $3.127 billion at 2024-12-31.
- Net debt fell from $21.799 billion at 2024-12-31 to $21.235 billion at 2025-12-31.
- Long-term debt was $24.140 billion at 2025-12-31, fairly stable relative to prior periods.

Still, liquidity remains tight in accounting terms, and negative equity means MO has limited balance-sheet flexibility if regulators, litigation, or business pressures were to intensify.

The asset mix also deserves scrutiny:

- Goodwill and other intangible assets were $17.663 billion at 2025-12-31.
- Investments and advances were $8.617 billion.
- Tangible book value was -$21.165 billion.

A large portion of the asset base is intangible or investment-related rather than hard operating assets. That is not unusual for MO, but it increases sensitivity to impairment charges and valuation resets, which has already shown up in the income statement.

MO’s 6.46% dividend yield is one of the stock’s central supports. Based on the provided data, the dividend appears sustainable in the near to medium term because:

**Bottom Line**

MO’s fundamentals support the view that it remains a high-cash-flow, shareholder-return-driven defensive equity with solid near-term earnings support and strong dividend coverage. The business itself appears stable. The main concern is not operational collapse, but financial structure: negative equity, weak liquidity metrics, and recurring impairment charges reduce balance-sheet flexibility and can generate periodic earnings volatility. For traders, MO is fundamentally attractive when the market rewards income, defensiveness, and cash flow durability. It is less attractive when the market becomes highly sensitive to leverage, accounting quality, or long-duration regulatory risk.
