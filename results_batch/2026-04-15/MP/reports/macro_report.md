Macro view for `MP` as of April 15, 2026: the US is in a late-cycle, soft-landing regime. Growth is still positive, labor remains resilient, and the Treasury curve has re-steepened into a normal upward slope, but inflation is not fully contained. Headline CPI rose 0.87% month over month in the latest print and PPI rose 1.78% month over month, which argues against an aggressive easing cycle. Unemployment at 4.3% and payroll growth of +178k indicate the labor market is cooling only gradually, not collapsing.

Policy direction is therefore likely “higher for a bit longer, then slow easing if disinflation resumes.” The effective fed funds rate fell from 4.09% on October 1, 2025 to 3.64% by January 1, 2026 and has been unchanged through March 1, 2026. That pause, alongside a 2Y-10Y spread of +0.50%, suggests the market is pricing ongoing expansion rather than imminent recession. For `MP`, that is moderately supportive from a demand/risk-appetite perspective, but the rise in long-end yields to 4.26% on the 10Y and 4.87% on the 30Y raises discount-rate pressure and keeps financing conditions from becoming easy.

Cross-asset implication for `MP`: macro is mildly constructive for a cyclical, commodity-linked equity if industrial demand expectations remain intact, but sticky upstream inflation and elevated long-duration yields create two-way risk. The main macro risks other analysts should incorporate are: 1) inflation re-acceleration forcing the Fed back into a hawkish stance, 2) long-end yields continuing higher and compressing equity multiples, and 3) the apparent soft landing weakening if payroll growth rolls over. Missing PMI, consumer confidence, and VIX data reduce confidence in the strength of the cyclical signal.

My macro stance on `MP` is **cautiously constructive / neutral-to-positive**, not an all-clear risk-on regime.
