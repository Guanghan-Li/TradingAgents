ServiceNow, Inc. (`NOW`) remains fundamentally strong on the latest available data through 2025-12-31, but the stock is trading as if growth quality or valuation durability is being repriced. The key setup for traders is a sharp disconnect: core operating metrics are still expanding at a healthy rate, cash generation is excellent, and the balance sheet is liquid, yet `NOW` is the weakest name in the provided peer set on 1-month, 3-month, 6-month, and YTD performance. That makes `NOW` a high-quality business in a very weak tape, not a broken business on the evidence provided.

`NOW` is an enterprise application software company with a subscription-heavy SaaS model, and that matters because deferred revenue, cash flow, and renewal/billing behavior are more informative than headline GAAP EPS alone. TTM revenue is $13.28B, gross profit is $10.30B, EBITDA is $2.74B, net income is $1.75B, and free cash flow is $4.95B. That implies very strong underlying unit economics: gross margin about 77.5%, EBITDA margin about 20.6%, operating margin 16.5%, net margin 13.2%, and FCF margin about 37.3%. Return on equity is 15.5% and return on assets is 5.4%, both consistent with a scaled software platform rather than an early-stage growth story.

The latest quarter was still solid on growth. Q4 2025 revenue was $3.568B versus $2.957B in Q4 2024, up about 20.7% year over year. Gross profit rose to $2.734B from $2.326B, up about 17.5%. Operating income increased to $443M from $374M, up about 18.4%. Net income rose to $401M from $384M, up about 4.4%. Diluted EPS increased to $0.38 from $0.366. Sequentially, revenue also grew from $3.407B in Q3 2025 to $3.568B in Q4 2025, up about 4.7%, although net income declined from $502M to $401M, showing that quarter-end profitability was pressured by higher operating costs, taxes, or integration spending even as top-line momentum remained healthy.

The best leading fundamental signal in the dataset is deferred revenue. Current deferred revenue reached $8.314B at 2025-12-31 versus $6.819B a year earlier, and total deferred revenue including non-current was about $8.434B versus about $6.914B a year earlier. That roughly 22% growth is important because it supports the view that `NOW` still has strong billings-like momentum and committed future revenue. Accounts receivable also rose to $2.627B from $2.240B year over year, consistent with larger enterprise billings activity.

Cash flow quality is strong, but traders should separate true cash strength from stock-based-compensation-supported optics. Q4 2025 operating cash flow was $2.238B and free cash flow was $2.000B, both very strong. Versus Q4 2024, operating cash flow increased from $1.635B and free cash flow from $1.372B. Over the latest four reported quarters, free cash flow sums to about $4.53B from the quarterly table, broadly consistent with the prefetched TTM figure of $4.95B. However, stock-based compensation was $494M in Q4 and about $1.96B over the last four quarters, which actually exceeds TTM net income of $1.75B. That means `NOW` is a strong cash compounder, but a meaningful part of the gap between GAAP earnings and cash generation is explained by non-cash compensation.

Capital allocation is active but mixed in effect. `NOW` repurchased $597M of stock in Q4 2025 and about $1.84B over the last four quarters. Treasury stock rose to $3.045B and treasury shares increased to 18.5M. But diluted share count was still 1.047B in Q4 2025 versus 1.045B in Q4 2024, so buybacks are largely offsetting compensation dilution rather than driving strong per-share shrinkage. Traders should not over-credit the buyback program as an EPS catalyst yet.

The balance sheet is healthy, though not as conservative as the company’s cash generation might suggest at first glance. `NOW` ended Q4 2025 with $6.284B in cash, cash equivalents, and short-term investments, plus $5.313B in investments and advances. Total debt was $2.403B, so the company is effectively in a net cash position on a broad liquidity view. Equity rose to $12.964B from $9.609B a year earlier. Still, current ratio is only about 1.00 and working capital was just $28M, so short-term liquidity looks tight on paper because deferred revenue and other current liabilities are large. For a subscription software company that is not automatically a red flag, but it does mean the liability side is doing a lot of work.

One major balance sheet change deserves attention: goodwill and other intangibles jumped to $4.699B at 2025-12-31 from $1.482B at 2024-12-31. Cash flow data shows $869M of business purchases in Q4 2025. This suggests `NOW` has been acquisitive, and the market may be discounting integration risk, margin pressure, or uncertainty around inorganic growth quality. This is one of the clearest fundamental changes in the dataset.

Valuation has reset, but `NOW` is not statistically cheap on trailing earnings. Market cap is about $98.5B. Against TTM revenue of $13.28B, the stock trades around 7.4x sales. Against TTM free cash flow of $4.95B, FCF yield is around 5.0%, which is not unreasonable for a scaled software leader. But trailing P/E is still 56.4x, while forward P/E is 18.8x. That spread implies the market expects a material earnings ramp. If `NOW` delivers that ramp, the current derating could reverse sharply. If not, the stock can stay under pressure despite strong revenue and cash flow.

Relative performance is the main warning signal. `NOW` is down 3.37% in the last week, 18.01% in the last month, 31.84% in the last three months, 49.01% in the last six months, and 36.12% YTD. Versus XLK, alpha is deeply negative across every window, including -26.45% over one month and -54.72% over six months. Among the provided peers, `NOW` ranks last over three months. This is not simple sector beta; it is severe stock-specific underperformance. Given the still-healthy reported fundamentals, traders should assume the market is worried about one or more of the following: multiple compression in long-duration software, acquisition/integration skepticism, margin durability, SBC dilution, or forward-guide credibility.

Macro is mildly supportive rather than hostile. The Fed funds rate is 3.64%, the curve is upward sloping, unemployment is 4.3%, and VIX is 18.36. That backdrop does not obviously explain `NOW`’s magnitude of underperformance by itself. In fact, a less restrictive rate environment should normally help premium software multiples. That strengthens the case that `NOW`’s recent selloff is more idiosyncratic than macro-driven.

Actionable trader readthrough for `NOW`: the business fundamentals still screen as high quality, especially revenue growth, deferred revenue growth, net cash liquidity, and free cash flow generation. The bearish case is not that the company is deteriorating outright; it is that quality is expensive, GAAP EPS is less impressive than FCF, acquisitions may complicate the story, and the tape is decisively against the stock. For tactical traders, that argues against assuming a bottom purely because the stock is down. For medium-term traders, the setup is more interesting: if the next update confirms durable 20% revenue growth, stable deferred revenue expansion, and improving EPS conversion, `NOW` could be a strong recovery candidate because expectations have clearly compressed. If instead margins soften further or acquisition synergies fail to appear, the weak relative trend is likely to persist.

Main bullish signals for `NOW`:
- Revenue still growing about 21% year over year in the latest quarter.
- Deferred revenue up about 22% year over year, supporting forward visibility.
- FCF remains very strong, with roughly 37% TTM FCF margin.
- Balance sheet remains liquid, with net cash support.
- Forward P/E is far below trailing P/E, implying a sizable expected earnings step-up.

Main bearish signals for `NOW`:
- Worst relative performer in the provided peer group.
- Trailing P/E still elevated at 56.4x despite the selloff.
- SBC is very large and exceeds TTM net income.
- Buybacks are not meaningfully reducing diluted shares.
- Goodwill/intangibles surged, pointing to acquisition and integration risk.
- Working capital and current ratio are tight, even if partly structural to SaaS.
