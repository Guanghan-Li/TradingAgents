Macro view for `NOW`: the backdrop is a soft-landing / mild re-acceleration regime, not a recession regime. Policy has eased materially from late 2025 to early 2026, with the effective fed funds rate down to 3.64%, but the Fed is now in a pause. At the same time, inflation is still running hot enough to keep long-end yields elevated: CPI rose 0.87% sequentially in March and PPI rose 1.78%, while the 10Y sits at 4.26% and the 30Y at 4.87%. Labor is still holding up, with unemployment at 4.3% and down sequentially. The curve is positively sloped, with 2Y-10Y at +50 bp, which argues against an imminent recession signal.

For `NOW`, that is a mixed but still workable macro setup. The positive side is that a non-inverted curve, stable labor market, and prior Fed easing support enterprise spending resilience and reduce near-term hard-landing risk. The negative side is that sticky inflation and a steepening long end keep duration pressure alive, which matters for `NOW` because high-quality software equities are still partly valued on long-duration cash flows. In practice, this means macro is supportive for `NOW` fundamentals but can cap multiple expansion if yields continue to rise.

Likely policy direction: the Fed looks biased toward an extended hold rather than an aggressive cutting cycle. The decline in policy rates into early 2026 suggests the central bank already delivered meaningful easing, and the recent inflation prints make a quick follow-on easing path less likely. Market interpretation should be: front-end anchored by Fed pause, back-end vulnerable to term premium and inflation persistence. That is usually constructive for cyclicals and credit if growth holds, but less cleanly supportive for expensive duration-heavy equities.

Cross-asset implications for `NOW`:
- Rates: the main macro variable to watch is the 10Y and long bond, not just the policy rate. If 10Y yields remain around 4.25% or move higher, `NOW` could see valuation compression even if operating trends stay solid.
- Credit: the current regime should be decent for IG/HY spreads as long as labor stays firm. Tight credit is indirectly supportive for enterprise software budgets and large transformation projects that benefit `NOW`.
- Equities: low VIX at 18.36 suggests risk appetite is still intact, which helps high-quality growth software. But if inflation surprises keep lifting the long end, leadership can rotate away from duration-sensitive software.
- FX / global demand: a higher-for-longer US yield structure can keep financial conditions tighter globally, which is a modest headwind for multinational software demand and deal timing.

Concrete risks the rest of the committee should incorporate for `NOW`:
- Inflation re-acceleration risk is the clearest macro threat. CPI and PPI both moved up sharply sequentially, which could delay cuts or even revive hiking fears at the long end.
- Long-duration valuation risk matters more than recession risk right now. `NOW` is more exposed to discount-rate repricing than to an immediate macro demand collapse.
- Data quality risk is non-trivial here because payrolls and ISM manufacturing were unavailable. If those missing series are weaker than the available data imply, the current soft-landing read may be overstating growth resilience.
- If unemployment starts rising from 4.3% while inflation stays sticky, that would create the worst mix for `NOW`: weaker enterprise spending and still-elevated yields.

Bottom line for `NOW`: macro is modestly constructive on demand but not cleanly bullish for valuation. The regime favors companies with strong execution and mission-critical budgets, but upside for `NOW` equity multiple likely depends on long-end yields stabilizing or falling. Other analysts should treat macro as a support for revenue durability, while keeping a clear haircut on valuation sensitivity to the 10Y and 30Y.
