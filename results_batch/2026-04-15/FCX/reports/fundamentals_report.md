FCX fundamental report as of April 15, 2026

Freeport-McMoRan Inc. (`FCX`) is a large-cap Basic Materials company in the copper industry with a market capitalization of about $98.7 billion. The stock is trading close to its 52-week high (`$69.75` high vs `~$68-69` implied current level from market cap/share count), which is consistent with very strong recent performance: `+5.53%` over 1 week, `+18.59%` over 1 month, `+16.06%` over 3 months, and `+61.57%` over 6 months. FCX ranks `1/6` among its peer set on 3-month performance and has materially outperformed the Materials ETF (`XLB`) across every reported period. The market is clearly pricing FCX as a high-beta cyclical winner in the current macro backdrop.

From a basic financial perspective, FCX’s trailing fundamentals are solid but not uniformly cheap. TTM revenue is `$25.9B`, gross profit is `$9.6B`, EBITDA is `$9.1B`, net income is `$2.20B`, and free cash flow is `$1.56B`. Profitability is respectable for a miner, with `14.4%` operating margin, `8.5%` net margin, `13.9%` ROE, and `7.2%` ROA. Liquidity is healthy with a `2.29` current ratio. The main valuation tension is that the stock looks expensive on trailing earnings but less demanding on forward earnings: TTM P/E is `45.2x`, while forward P/E is `17.1x`, and forward EPS (`4.03`) is far above trailing EPS (`1.52`). That gap implies the market expects a substantial earnings rebound, likely tied to commodity price strength, better volumes, or both. Traders should read this as a stock where expectations already matter almost as much as current fundamentals.

The quarterly income statement shows why the valuation debate is mixed. Through 2025, FCX produced strong mid-year earnings but a softer fourth quarter. Revenue moved from `$5.73B` in Q1 2025 to `$7.58B` in Q2, `$6.97B` in Q3, and then down to `$5.63B` in Q4. Net income attributable to common stock followed a similar pattern: `$352M`, `$772M`, `$674M`, then `$406M`. EBITDA also stepped down from `$2.66B` in Q3 to `$1.36B` in Q4. Q4 2025 therefore showed a clear deceleration versus Q2-Q3 2025. Gross profit fell from `$2.14B` in Q3 to `$1.02B` in Q4, and operating income dropped from `$1.96B` to `$811M`. For traders, the key read-through is that FCX remains highly earnings-sensitive to realized pricing, shipment mix, and cost absorption. The business is profitable, but quarterly earnings power is volatile.

A critical nuance in FCX’s reported earnings is the size of noncontrolling interests. At December 31, 2025, minority interest on the balance sheet was `$11.87B`, versus common equity of `$18.90B`. On the income statement, consolidated net income including noncontrolling interests was often materially higher than net income attributable to common shareholders. For example, in Q2 2025, consolidated net income was `$1.55B`, but net income to common was `$772M`. This matters because a casual read of consolidated operating strength can overstate what ultimately accrues to FCX equity holders. Traders should prioritize attributable EPS and attributable free cash flow rather than only consolidated EBITDA.

Balance sheet quality is a relative strength. Total assets increased to `$58.17B` at December 31, 2025 from `$54.85B` a year earlier, while stockholders’ equity rose to `$18.90B` from `$17.58B`. Retained earnings improved sharply from negative `$170M` at December 31, 2024 to positive `$1.39B` at December 31, 2025, showing that the company rebuilt equity internally despite a capital-intensive year. Cash and equivalents were `$3.82B`, total debt was `$10.49B`, and net debt was `$5.56B`. Against TTM EBITDA of `$9.10B`, net debt is only about `0.6x EBITDA`, which is manageable for a cyclical miner. That means leverage is not the immediate issue; the bigger debate is operating execution and commodity price realization.

Working capital and asset trends are worth monitoring. Inventory rose from `$6.81B` at December 31, 2024 to `$7.49B` at December 31, 2025, and receivables also increased. Working capital remained strong at `$7.77B`, so liquidity is not stressed, but the inventory build suggests some combination of production growth, shipment timing, or slower monetization. That is not automatically negative, but if inventory keeps rising without matching revenue acceleration, it could pressure cash conversion. Net property, plant, and equipment climbed to `$40.74B` from `$38.51B`, confirming that FCX is still in a heavy reinvestment cycle.

Cash flow confirms both the strength and the limitation of the story. Operating cash flow was strong through most of 2025: `$1.06B` in Q1, `$2.20B` in Q2, `$1.66B` in Q3, then down to `$693M` in Q4. Capital expenditures were consistently heavy at roughly `$1.0B-$1.26B` each quarter. As a result, free cash flow was uneven: `-$114M` in Q1, `+$934M` in Q2, `+$608M` in Q3, and `-$312M` in Q4. The company still generated positive TTM free cash flow, but Q4 showed how quickly FCF can turn negative when operating cash flow softens while capex stays elevated. FCX therefore remains fundamentally investable, but not yet a clean “cash machine” story at current spending levels.

Capital allocation was balanced rather than aggressive. Dividends paid were stable around `$215M-$218M` per quarter, supporting a modest `0.88%` dividend yield. Debt repayments were substantial throughout 2025, with gross repayments of `$639M` to `$971M` per quarter, partly offset by new issuance. Share repurchases were minimal. This suggests management is still prioritizing project funding and balance sheet flexibility over large-scale shareholder returns. For traders, that makes sense in a constructive copper environment, but it also means the equity case relies more on earnings and commodity upside than on buyback-driven EPS growth.

The macro backdrop is broadly supportive for FCX. As of March 1, 2026, the effective federal funds rate was `3.64%`, down from `4.09%` on October 1, 2025. The Treasury curve on April 14, 2026 was upward sloping, with `2Y at 3.76%` and `10Y at 4.26%`, indicating more normal growth expectations rather than recession pricing. Unemployment was `4.3%`, payrolls were still growing, and market volatility was moderate (`VIX 18.36` on April 14, 2026). For a cyclical materials name like FCX, that is a favorable backdrop. Inflation data is still firm, however: CPI rose `0.87%` sequentially and PPI rose `1.78%` sequentially in the latest readings, so input-cost and policy-risk sensitivity has not disappeared.

The most important near-term interpretation is that FCX’s stock strength currently exceeds the improvement visible in recent quarterly cash generation. The shares have dramatically outperformed the sector ETF and peers, but Q4 2025 fundamentals were weaker than Q2-Q3 2025 across revenue, EBITDA, operating cash flow, and free cash flow. My inference from the provided data is that the market is looking through a soft quarter and discounting stronger 2026 earnings. That is consistent with the large gap between trailing and forward P/E. If the next earnings cycle validates that rebound, FCX still has room to work. If not, the stock is exposed because it is already priced near a 52-week high and carries a `1.47` beta.

Actionable insights for traders:

1. FCX is financially healthy enough to support a bullish cyclical thesis. Liquidity is good, leverage is manageable, and TTM EBITDA is strong.
2. The key operating watch item is cash conversion, not just revenue growth. Q4 2025 free cash flow turned negative despite positive earnings because capex stayed high and working capital absorbed cash.
3. Attributable earnings matter more than consolidated earnings because noncontrolling interests are large.
4. The valuation setup says the market expects recovery: `45.2x` trailing P/E vs `17.1x` forward P/E. That supports upside if 2026 numbers inflect, but it also raises downside risk if copper or volumes disappoint.
5. Momentum is exceptional, but that cuts both ways. FCX has outperformed `XLB` by `+6.24%` over 1 week, `+14.12%` over 1 month, and `+43.98%` over 6 months. Expectations are elevated.
6. The stock is better suited to traders seeking commodity-linked upside than to income-focused investors. Dividend yield is low, and the story remains cyclical growth plus operating leverage.
